Roger Ebert's film review style combined **emotional honesty** with **intellectual rigor**, prioritizing a film's artistry over its ideas while structuring reviews to immerse readers in its sensory and thematic experience.[1][2]

### Tone
Ebert adopted a conversational yet analytical tone that emphasized subjective emotional response alongside objective critique. He stressed that reviewers should validate personal feelings about a film but back them with reasoned analysis, avoiding mere praise for "correct" ideas or attacks for being "wrong."[2] His writing often celebrated film's unique capacities for escapism, voyeurism, sensuality, and nonverbal immersion, evoking a shared "soul/consciousness" between filmmaker and viewer.[1]

### Structure
Reviews typically immersed readers in the film's personalities and perspectives before broader analysis. Ebert described non-traditional films as fluid experiences "inside" the narrative, turning between viewpoints without rigid conventions, then transitioned to evaluating how artistry served (or failed) thematic or idea-driven elements.[1] This created a narrative flow mirroring film's flexibility, distinct from written argumentation suited to abstract debate.[1]

### Approach to Themes
Ebert analyzed themes through film's strengths in conveying mood, settings, human emotions, and experiential "being there," rather than logical arguments. He argued ideas in films succeed as occasions for artistry—e.g., Godard's political films excel via visuals like a setting sun or facial expressions, not abstract reasoning—urging focus on sensory and immersive qualities over ideological correctness.[1]
