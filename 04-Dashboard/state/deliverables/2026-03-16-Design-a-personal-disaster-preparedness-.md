# Current Best Practices in Family Disaster Preparedness (2026)

## Emergency Supply Lists

**Water and Food Foundation**

Store **1 gallon of water per person per day** for drinking and sanitation in sealed, unbreakable containers, renewing the supply every six months[1]. Current expert recommendations have evolved beyond the traditional 72-hour minimum: FEMA and the Red Cross recommend a minimum 72-hour supply, but most professionals now recommend **2 weeks (14 days) of water and food** as the preparedness foundation[2]. For food, maintain a **2,000 calorie per person per day supply** of non-perishable items requiring no cooking, such as canned fruits and vegetables, crackers, peanut butter, and dry cereal[1][2].

**Core Supplies Checklist**

Essential items include: manual can opener, flashlights with extra batteries, first aid kit, emergency blankets, and portable phone chargers/power banks[1]. Additional supplies recommended by the Red Cross include battery-powered or hand-crank radio, extra batteries, multi-purpose tool, sanitation and personal hygiene items, maps of the area, emergency whistle (one per family member), and a change of clothes[3]. For specific emergencies, include a **one-month supply of medications and medical supplies**[3], feminine hygiene products (2-week supply), and comfort items like books and board games for children[2].

**Go-Bag vs. Stay-at-Home Kit**

Organize supplies into two separate kits: a **go-kit with 72-hour supplies** that you can carry with you and phone/device chargers, and a **stay-at-home kit with two weeks of food and water**[3]. Keep your go-bag packed near your front door for immediate evacuation readiness[2].

## Communication Plans

**Family Communication Strategy**

Designate **an out-of-state friend or relative as a central point of contact**, as it is often easier to call long-distance than within a disaster zone[1]. Establish **two meeting places**—one right outside your home in case of sudden emergencies like fire, and another outside your neighborhood in case you cannot return home[1]. Write down important phone numbers on a physical contact card and carry it with you, as phone and internet may be down[3].

**Tools and Monitoring**

Use a **NOAA weather radio with S.A.M.E. technology** (Midland ER310 recommended) to monitor local weather and news reports even if power goes out[2]. The Red Cross provides an official **Ready.gov Make a Plan Form** to create and email a PDF of your Family Emergency Communication Plan to all members[1]. Practice your family communication plan and evacuation routes **twice per year**, as plans fail without rehearsal[2].

## Evacuation Routes and Meeting Points

Establish clear evacuation routes from your home and neighborhood, with physical paper road maps for your state and region stored in your emergency kit[2]. Document your two designated meeting places—one near your home and one outside your neighborhood—and ensure all family members know these locations[1]. Sign up for free emergency alerts from your local government to receive advance warning of potential evacuations[3].

## Financial Document Protection

Store copies of IDs, insurance documents, medical records, and a list of emergency contacts in a **waterproof, fireproof container**[1]. The IRS recommends this approach specifically to protect tax records[1]. Keep your personal, financial, and medical records in a safe place that's easy to access, ideally making both hard copies and secure electronic backups[3]. Include **cash in small bills ($100–200)** in your emergency kit, as ATMs may be down during disasters[2]. Carry a small card listing your medications and dosages for medical reference[3].

## 72-Hour Self-Sufficiency and Extended Preparedness

**Immediate 72-Hour Readiness**

Your go-kit should enable complete self-sufficiency for 72 hours with water, food, first aid supplies, communication devices, and essential tools[3]. Include battery backups for phones and a portable phone charger/power bank[1].

**Power and Shelter Solutions**

For extended emergencies, a **portable solar generator** (such as the EcoFlow Delta 2 Max) eliminates fuel-dependency and powers essentials for multiple days[2]. Identify one interior room (with fewer windows and no fireplace) that can be sealed for air quality events, stocking it with an air purifier, sleeping supplies, portable solar generator, water and food for 48–72 hours, and your first aid kit[2]. Practice sealing the room with weatherstripping tape[2].

**Customization for Household Needs**

Tailor your kits for specific family members: include bottles, formula, wipes, and diapers for young children; leashes, carriers, food, bowls, and photos for pets in case of separation; and specialized medical supplies such as hearing aids with extra batteries, glasses, contact lenses, and syringes[3].

## Disaster Preparedness Plan for a Family of Four

### Emergency Supply Lists

#### Water and Food Foundation
- **Water Supply**: Store 1 gallon of water per person per day, totaling 56 gallons for a two-week period for a family of four. Use sealed, unbreakable containers and renew every six months[1][2].
- **Food Supply**: Maintain a 2,000 calorie per person per day supply of non-perishable foods. This includes canned fruits, vegetables, crackers, peanut butter, and dry cereal. Total food supply should cover 14 days[1][2].

#### Core Supplies Checklist
- **Basic Essentials**:
  - Manual can opener
  - Flashlights with extra batteries
  - First aid kit
  - Emergency blankets
  - Portable phone chargers/power banks[1]
- **Additional Supplies**:
  - Battery-powered or hand-crank radio
  - Extra batteries
  - Multi-purpose tool
  - Sanitation and personal hygiene items
  - Maps of the area
  - Emergency whistle (one per family member)
  - Change of clothes[3]
- **Medical and Comfort Items**:
  - One-month supply of medications and medical supplies
  - Feminine hygiene products (2-week supply)
  - Comfort items like books and board games for children[2]

#### Go-Bag vs. Stay-at-Home Kit
- **Go-Bag**: Prepare a portable kit with 72-hour supplies for each family member. Include essentials like water, food, flashlight, first aid kit, and personal hygiene items.
- **Stay-at-Home Kit**: Keep a more extensive supply at home for long-term self-sufficiency (14 days)[1][2].

### Communication Plan
- **Family Communication Strategy**:
  - Establish a primary and secondary meeting location outside the home.
  - Designate an out-of-town contact person for all family members to check in with.
  - Use text messaging as the primary form of communication due to potential network congestion.
- **Emergency Contacts**:
  - Compile a list of emergency contacts including family, friends, local authorities, and medical facilities. Ensure all family members have access to this list.

### Evacuation Routes
- **Identify and Map Routes**:
  - Identify at least two evacuation routes from your home to a safe location.
  - Practice these routes with all family members, considering potential obstacles such as traffic or road closures.
- **Local Shelter Information**:
  - Research and list local shelters and their capacities.
  - Keep a map with these locations marked in your emergency kit.

### Financial Document Protection Strategies
- **Document Safeguarding**:
  - Store copies of important documents (IDs, insurance policies, bank account information) in a waterproof and fireproof safe.
  - Digitize documents and store them securely in the cloud for easy access.
- **Emergency Funds**:
  - Maintain a small cash reserve in small denominations as ATMs and banks may not be accessible.

### 72-Hour Self-Sufficiency Protocols
- **Immediate Actions**:
  - Ensure go-bags are easily accessible.
  - Keep vehicles fueled and ready for evacuation.
  - Regularly review and update the emergency plan with all family members.

### First Steps
1. **Inventory Check**: Conduct an inventory of current supplies and identify gaps.
2. **Supply Acquisition**: Purchase necessary items to complete both the go-bag and stay-at-home kit.
3. **Communication Plan Setup**: Discuss and finalize the communication plan with all family members.
4. **Route Practice**: Conduct a practice evacuation using identified routes.
