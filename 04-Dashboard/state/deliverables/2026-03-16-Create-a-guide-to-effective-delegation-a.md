## Research Challenges and Best Practices
## Challenges for New Engineering Managers in First 90 Days

**82% of new managers receive no formal training, leading to uncertainty in transitioning from individual contributor (IC) to manager roles.** This gap often results in unmanaged expectations, with promoted engineers lacking guidance on core responsibilities like team leadership and performance management.[1]

**Common issues include poor onboarding, causing higher turnover and lower productivity; 76% of workplaces fail to onboard new hires properly, and only 47% see effective retention from onboarding programs.** New managers face frustrations like unclear expectations in the first month, radio silence post-offer, and overemphasis on paperwork (40% of activities), eroding new hire excitement and increasing anxiety.[4]

**Team management challenges peak with overload; research recommends 5–9 direct reports per manager to avoid burnout, as exceeding this impairs scaling and team health.** Holiday periods exacerbate this with staggered PTO and deadlines, disrupting rhythm.[1]

**Burnout detection is critical but subtle; early signals in behavioral data often go unnoticed until disengagement or attrition hits.**[1]

## Best Practices for First 90 Days

**Structure onboarding with 30-60-90 day plans: send personalized tracks at days 30, 60, and 90, including team-specific details, manager names, and new hire checklists to standardize processes and build culture.** Pair with pulse surveys within 48 hours of announcements to gauge comprehension; top-down signaling makes it mandatory to ensure completion despite manager time constraints.[2][3][4]

**Prioritize 1:1 meetings as the core tool for team health: use AI insights for richer, actionable conversations on growth and performance; schedule weekly or bi-weekly check-ins.** Focus on feedback loops—add reactions to newsletters, unstructured Q&A, and "You asked, we answered" sections.[1][2]

**Adopt time management templates: use weekly schedules to prioritize tasks by deadlines and team objectives; implement Pomodoro for daily focus to combat procrastination.** For onboarding, organize documents and training via templates aligned with 30-60-90 plans.[3]

**Implement OKRs gradually: run a pilot with 2-3 teams for one quarter, appoint an OKR champion for training, then expand; conduct quarterly planning, weekly check-ins on Key Results, and reviews.** This aligns teams without overwhelming new managers.[5]

**Build communication toolkit: define goals per message (understand/feel/do), measure with pulse surveys and manager feedback; personalize for groups like remote employees.** Offer flexible hours and conditional remote work (e.g., 2 days/week) for retention without pay increases.[2][4]

## Key Findings

1. **Lack of Training and Onboarding**: 82% of new managers do not receive formal training, leading to uncertainty in their roles. Poor onboarding practices affect 76% of workplaces, contributing to high turnover and low productivity. [1][4]

2. **Team Management Overload**: Optimal team size for direct reports is 5–9 to prevent burnout and maintain team health. Exceeding this range can lead to managerial overload and reduced effectiveness. [1]

3. **Burnout and Attrition**: Early detection of burnout is crucial but often missed, leading to disengagement and attrition. [1]

4. **Onboarding Best Practices**: Structured onboarding plans (30-60-90 days) and personalized tracks enhance new hire integration and cultural alignment. [1]

## Recommendations

1. **Implement Structured Onboarding**: Develop a 30-60-90 day onboarding plan tailored for new managers. This should include personalized tracks with team-specific information and manager introductions to foster a sense of belonging and clarity.

   - **Why**: Structured onboarding reduces turnover and increases productivity by setting clear expectations and providing necessary support.
   - **Expected Outcome**: Improved retention rates and faster integration into the team.
   - **First Step**: Draft a template for a 30-60-90 day plan, incorporating feedback from current managers.

2. **Optimize Team Size**: Ensure new managers have 5–9 direct reports to maintain effective oversight and prevent burnout.

   - **Why**: Managing an optimal number of direct reports enhances focus and team health, preventing managerial overload.
   - **Expected Outcome**: Increased managerial effectiveness and team performance.
   - **First Step**: Review current team structures and adjust as necessary to align with best practices.

3. **Monitor Burnout Indicators**: Implement tools and processes to detect early signs of burnout among managers and their teams.

   - **Why**: Early detection of burnout can prevent disengagement and reduce attrition rates.
   - **Expected Outcome**: Healthier work environment and reduced turnover.
   - **First Step**: Introduce regular pulse surveys and behavioral data analysis to identify early burnout signals.

4. **Provide Managerial Training**: Develop a training program focused on transition skills from IC to manager, covering team leadership and performance management.

   - **Why**: Formal training equips new managers with the skills needed to succeed in their roles.
   - **Expected Outcome**: Increased confidence and competence in new managers.
   - **First Step**: Identify key training areas and resources, and schedule regular training sessions.
