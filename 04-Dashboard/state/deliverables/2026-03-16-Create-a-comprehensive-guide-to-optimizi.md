## Access Point Placement & Coverage

**Router placement is critical for signal strength.** Position your router centrally in your home, elevated off the ground, and away from physical obstructions like thick walls[2]. For larger homes (2,000+ sq ft), a single router often creates dead zones in distant rooms[5].

**Mesh Wi-Fi systems eliminate dead zones** by using multiple nodes to distribute signal throughout your home, enabling seamless device movement without connection drops[4]. The **Netgear Orbi 770** is recommended for large households, employing multiple nodes to eliminate Wi-Fi dead zones[4]. The **Eero Max 7** is optimized for smart homes, managing dozens of connected devices without performance degradation[4].

For homes where mesh systems aren't sufficient, **powerline networking kits** use your electrical wiring to create wired network connections in areas traditional Wi-Fi cannot reach[1].

## Channel & Frequency Selection

**Use 5 GHz instead of 2.4 GHz for remote work.** The 5 GHz band offers faster speeds and lower interference, though 2.4 GHz travels better through walls and floors[2]. For optimal performance, disable or rate-limit 2.4 GHz on your router and configure all work devices to use 5 GHz exclusively[2].

**Wi-Fi 7 routers** are now replacing older standards, increasing data transfer speeds, reducing latency, and handling multiple devices simultaneously[4]. For large homes with multiple floors, **tri-band routers** (operating on 2.4 GHz, 5 GHz, and 6 GHz) provide faster speeds than dual-band alternatives[5].

## Quality of Service (QoS) Configuration

**Prioritize work-critical activities** using your router's QoS feature. Configure settings to ensure video conferencing and large file transfers receive priority bandwidth[1]. This prevents non-work devices from consuming bandwidth during important meetings.

**Create a dedicated guest network** exclusively for work devices if your router supports it, segregating work traffic from household devices and reducing bandwidth competition[1].

## Advanced Router Features

Enable **Beamforming and MU-MIMO (Multi-User, Multiple Input, Multiple Output)** technologies in your router's advanced settings to enhance signal strength and reliability, especially with multiple connected devices[1].

Use **network analyzing tools** like Wi-Fi Analyzer or inSSIDer to identify signal strength throughout your home, pinpoint dead zones, and detect interference sources[1].

## Troubleshooting Common Problems

**Regularly reboot your modem and router** to improve performance and speed[2]. This simple step resolves many connectivity issues.

**Disable unused devices and applications** consuming bandwidth during work hours[2]. Remember that Wi-Fi is a shared-access medium—the total number of connected devices directly affects performance.

**Switch to Ethernet for critical work.** Wired connections via Ethernet cable provide faster, more stable performance than Wi-Fi, making them ideal for video conferencing[1][2]. This eliminates wireless interference but limits device mobility.

**Consider fiber-optic providers** (CenturyLink, US Internet, Google Fiber, Jaguar Communications Fiber) for the highest quality connections suitable for remote work[2]. Cable TV providers may share neighborhood bandwidth, potentially capping available speeds in smaller communities[2].

**Upgrade to newer Wi-Fi access points or mesh networks** if your current equipment is outdated. The New York Times' Wirecutter regularly tests and recommends Wi-Fi access points and mesh hardware[2].
