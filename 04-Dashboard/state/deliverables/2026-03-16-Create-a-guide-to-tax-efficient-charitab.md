## Donor-Advised Funds (DAFs)
DAFs enable donors to make a large upfront contribution for an immediate tax deduction while distributing grants over time, ideal for bunching strategies under 2026 rules with a new 0.5% AGI floor on itemized deductions. DAFs accept appreciated assets like securities, real estate, or business interests, avoiding capital gains tax; however, DAF contributions do not qualify for the new above-the-line deduction for non-itemizers.[1][3][4]

## Appreciated Stock Donations
Donating long-term appreciated securities (held >1 year) directly to qualified charities allows deduction of full fair market value without recognizing capital gains tax, more efficient than selling and donating proceeds; deductible up to 30% of AGI with 5-year carryforward.[2][3][4] Instruct brokers for direct transfers from non-retirement accounts to avoid gains on unrealized appreciation.[2]

## Bunching Strategies
Bunch 2-3 years of donations into one year via DAFs to exceed the standard deduction plus 0.5% AGI floor (e.g., first ~$1,000 nondeductible for most itemizers), enabling itemization; cash gifts to public charities now permanent at 60% AGI max (post-floor).[1][2][3][4][5] Itemized deductions capped at 35% effective value even for 37% bracket taxpayers.[4][5]

## Above-the-Line Deduction for Non-Itemizers
New under OBBBA: Cash gifts qualify for up to $1,000 (single) or $2,000 (joint) above-the-line deduction without itemizing, effective 2026; excludes non-cash or DAF gifts.[1][3][4]

## Qualified Charitable Distributions (QCDs)
For age 70½+: Transfer up to $111,000 ($222,000 joint) directly from IRA to charities in 2026, excludes from taxable income, satisfies RMDs, bypasses AGI floor; rollovers from 401(k)/403(b) allowed first.[1][2][3][4][6]

## Analysis

### Donor-Advised Funds (DAFs)
- **Functionality**: DAFs allow donors to make a large upfront contribution, receive an immediate tax deduction, and distribute grants over time. This strategy is particularly effective for bunching donations to exceed the standard deduction under the 2026 tax rules, which introduce a 0.5% AGI floor on itemized deductions.
- **Tax Efficiency**: Contributions of appreciated assets to DAFs avoid capital gains tax. However, these contributions do not qualify for the new above-the-line deduction for non-itemizers.
- **Example**: If a donor contributes $50,000 in appreciated stock to a DAF, they avoid capital gains tax on the appreciation and can deduct the full $50,000, assuming it exceeds the AGI floor and standard deduction.

### Appreciated Stock Donations
- **Benefits**: Donating appreciated securities directly to a charity allows for a deduction of the full market value without incurring capital gains tax. This is deductible up to 30% of AGI with a 5-year carryforward.
- **Example**: Donating $20,000 in appreciated stock with a cost basis of $10,000 avoids capital gains tax on the $10,000 appreciation and allows a deduction of $20,000, provided it does not exceed 30% of AGI.

### Bunching Strategies
- **Mechanism**: By bunching 2-3 years of donations into one year, donors can exceed the standard deduction threshold, making itemizing more beneficial.
- **Example**: Instead of donating $10,000 annually, a donor could contribute $30,000 in one year to a DAF, allowing for itemization that year and standard deduction in subsequent years.

## Comparison
- **DAFs vs. Appreciated Stock Donations**: DAFs offer flexibility in grant timing and can accept a variety of asset types, while appreciated stock donations provide immediate tax benefits without the need for a fund structure.
- **Bunching vs. Regular Contributions**: Bunching maximizes deductions in high-donation years, while regular contributions might not exceed the standard deduction, reducing tax efficiency.

## Recommendation
1. **Utilize DAFs for Bunching**: Establish a DAF to bunch donations and exceed the standard deduction, especially under the 2026 rules.
   - **Why**: Provides immediate tax deduction and flexibility in distribution.
   - **Expected Outcome**: Maximized tax benefits in high-donation years.
   - **First Step**: Open a DAF account with a financial institution or community foundation.

2. **Donate Appreciated Stocks**: Directly transfer appreciated stocks to charities to avoid capital gains tax.
   - **Why**: More tax-efficient than cash donations.
   - **Expected Outcome**: Full market value deduction and capital gains avoidance.
   - **First Step**: Coordinate with a broker to facilitate direct stock transfers to chosen charities.

3. **Implement Bunching Strategy**: Plan donations to bunch in specific years, leveraging DAFs for timing flexibility.
   - **Why**: To exceed the standard deduction and optimize tax savings.
   - **Expected Outcome**: Increased tax deductions in targeted years.
   - **First Step**: Analyze past donation patterns and forecast future giving to identify optimal bunching years.

## Risks & Disclaimers
- **Market Volatility**: The value of appreciated stocks can fluctuate, affecting the timing and amount of deductions.
- **Regulatory Changes**: Future tax law changes could impact the effectiveness of these strategies.
- **Complexity**: Managing DAFs and stock donations requires careful planning and coordination with financial advisors and tax professionals.
