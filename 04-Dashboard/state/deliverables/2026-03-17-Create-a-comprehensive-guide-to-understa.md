# SECURE 2.0 Act Changes to Required Minimum Distributions

## Finding 1: RMD Starting Age Increased to 73, with Future Increase to 75

The SECURE 2.0 Act, signed into law in late 2022, raised the **required minimum distribution (RMD) starting age from 72 to 73** for individuals reaching age 72 after December 31, 2022.[4] The RMD age will increase again to **75 in 2033** for individuals born in 1960 or later.[4][5] This change applies to both traditional IRAs and employer-sponsored retirement plans like 401(k)s.

The specific timeline depends on birth year:[1]
- Individuals born before 1951: RMD age remains 72
- Individuals born 1951-1959: RMD age is 73
- Individuals born 1960 or later: RMD age is 75 (effective 2033)

**Source:** https://www.irafinancial.com/blog/solo-401k-rmd-rules-secure-act-2-0/

## Finding 2: Designated Roth Accounts Eliminated from Lifetime RMD Requirements

Under SECURE 2.0, **designated Roth accounts in employer-sponsored plans are now exempt from lifetime RMD requirements.**[4][5][6] This aligns Roth 401(k)s with Roth IRAs, which have never required lifetime distributions. Distributions from designated Roth accounts do not count toward satisfying the plan's overall RMD requirement for the year.[2]

However, this exemption applies only to the original account owner during their lifetime. Non-spouse beneficiaries who inherit Roth accounts may still face RMD requirements.[2]

**Source:** https://www.currentfederaltaxdevelopments.com/blog/2026/2/23/update-on-required-minimum-distribution-regulations-impact-of-irs-announcement-2026-7

## Finding 3: Excise Tax on Missed RMDs Reduced from 50% to 10%

SECURE 2.0 significantly reduced penalties for missed RMDs. The **excise tax on missed RMDs was reduced from 50% to 10%** if the shortfall is corrected within a specified timeframe.[5][2] For example, missing a $10,000 RMD previously resulted in a $5,000 penalty; under the new rules, the penalty would be $1,000 if corrected.[5]

**Source:** https://www.kavout.com/market-lens/what-s-driving-the-latest-rmd-changes-for-2026

## Finding 4: 10-Year Distribution Rule for Non-Spouse Beneficiaries

The SECURE 2.0 Act confirmed that **most non-spouse beneficiaries must withdraw the entire inherited IRA within 10 years**, with annual required minimum distributions potentially applying during that 10-year window.[3] This replaced the previous "stretch IRA" rules that allowed beneficiaries to extend distributions over their lifetime.

**Source:** https://gravislaw.com/tax-law/secure-2-0-inherited-ira-rules-2026/

## Finding 5: Surviving Spouse Distribution Rules Improved

When an employee dies before their required beginning date and the surviving spouse is the sole beneficiary, the spouse is now **automatically treated as the employee for RMD purposes without requiring an election.**[8] The spouse uses the Uniform Lifetime Table rather than the Single Life Table, resulting in longer distribution periods and smaller annual withdrawals.

**Source:** https://whipplewood.com/insights/new-rmd-proposals-retirement-withdrawals/

## Finding 6: IRS Delayed Final RMD Regulations Until 2027

The IRS issued **Announcement 2026-7** in February 2026, delaying the effective date of final RMD regulations until **no earlier than 2027** (originally scheduled for the 2026 distribution calendar year).[7] Until final regulations take effect, taxpayers must apply reasonable, good-faith interpretations of the statutory provisions.[7]

**Source:** https://www.boutwellfay.com/post/new-rmd-regulations-delayed-until-2027

## Finding 7: Higher "Cash Out" Threshold for Automatic Distributions

SECURE 2.0 increased the dollar threshold for immediate lump-sum distributions without participant consent from **$5,000 to $7,000.**[1] This applies to distributions following qualifying events and automatic rollovers of mandatory distributions.

**Source:** https://www.mjcpa.com/irs-updates-model-notices-for-retirement-plan-rollovers/

## Synthesize Guide on RMDs under SECURE Act 2.0
## Analysis

### RMD Starting Age Changes

- **Current RMD Age**: The SECURE 2.0 Act increased the RMD starting age from 72 to 73 for individuals reaching age 72 after December 31, 2022. This affects individuals born between 1951 and 1959. [Source](https://www.irafinancial.com/blog/solo-401k-rmd-rules-secure-act-2-0/)
- **Future RMD Age**: The RMD age will further increase to 75 in 2033 for those born in 1960 or later. This allows for a longer period of tax-deferred growth. [Source](https://www.irafinancial.com/blog/solo-401k-rmd-rules-secure-act-2-0/)

### Roth Accounts Exemption

- **Roth 401(k) Exemption**: Designated Roth accounts in employer-sponsored plans are now exempt from lifetime RMD requirements, aligning them with Roth IRAs. This means account holders can let their investments grow tax-free without mandatory withdrawals during their lifetime. [Source](https://www.currentfederaltaxdevelopments.com/blog/2026/2/23/update)
- **Beneficiary Considerations**: Non-spouse beneficiaries may still be subject to RMDs, ensuring that funds are eventually distributed and taxed. [Source](https://www.currentfederaltaxdevelopments.com/blog/2026/2/23/update)

## Comparison

- **Traditional IRAs vs. Roth Accounts**: Traditional IRAs still require RMDs starting at the specified age (73 or 75), whereas Roth IRAs and Roth 401(k)s do not require lifetime RMDs for the original owner.
- **Impact on Retirement Planning**: The delay in RMDs allows for extended tax-deferred growth, which can significantly impact retirement planning strategies, especially for those who do not need to draw on these funds immediately.

## Recommendation

### What to Do
