## Backdoor Roth IRA Strategy Overview
The **backdoor Roth IRA** enables high-income earners to bypass 2026 Roth IRA direct contribution income limits (not specified in results but contributions capped at **$7,500** or **$8,600** if 50+) by making nondeductible contributions to a traditional IRA and immediately converting to a Roth IRA, as conversions have no income limits.[3][1][4]

## Step-by-Step Execution for High Earners
- **Step 1**: Contribute after-tax (nondeductible) dollars to a traditional IRA up to the annual limit ($7,500/$8,600 in 2026).[3]
- **Step 2**: Convert the contribution to a Roth IRA shortly after (same day or pay period ideal) to minimize taxable earnings accrual.[2][4]
- **Step 3**: File **IRS Form 8606** with your tax return to report the nondeductible basis and conversion; prior-year basis from 2024 Form 8606 line 14 must be tracked if applicable, with a **$50 penalty** for non-filing (waivable for reasonable cause). Mail standalone 2024 Form 8606 if needed without amending return.[1][3]
- **TurboTax Entry**: On "Review your 1099-R info" screen (search 1099-R), answer **Yes** to prior nondeductible contributions, input basis and IRA values.[1]

## Pro-Rata Rule Impact and Mitigation
The **pro-rata rule** taxes conversions proportionally across all traditional, SEP, and SIMPLE IRAs based on pre-tax vs. after-tax balances; existing pre-tax IRAs make backdoor conversions partially taxable, derailing the strategy for many high earners.[4][5][3]
- Roll pre-tax IRAs into an employer 401(k) before converting to isolate the nondeductible contribution.[4][5]
- Calculate total IRA basis beforehand; applies to 401(k) conversions too.[5]

## Analysis

The backdoor Roth IRA strategy is a useful tool for high-income earners to bypass the income limits for Roth IRA contributions. Here are the key elements and numbers involved:

- **Contribution Limits**: As of 2026, the contribution limit for a traditional IRA is $7,500, or $8,600 if you are 50 or older. This is the maximum amount you can contribute in after-tax dollars before converting to a Roth IRA. [3]
- **Conversion Timing**: To minimize taxable earnings, it is recommended to convert the contribution to a Roth IRA immediately, ideally on the same day or within the same pay period. [2][4]
- **IRS Form 8606**: Essential for reporting the nondeductible contribution and conversion. Failing to file can result in a $50 penalty, although this can be waived for reasonable cause. [1][3]

## Comparison

- **Traditional IRA vs. Roth IRA**: Traditional IRAs allow for tax-deferred growth, but withdrawals are taxed. Roth IRAs offer tax-free growth and withdrawals. The backdoor strategy combines these benefits by allowing high-income earners to fund a Roth IRA despite income restrictions.
- **Direct Roth Contributions vs. Backdoor Conversion**: Direct contributions are limited by income; the backdoor method circumvents this by using a traditional IRA as a conduit.

   - **Why**: This allows you to legally bypass income limits and enjoy tax-free growth and withdrawals in retirement.
   - **Expected Outcome**: You will maximize your retirement savings potential by leveraging the Roth IRA's benefits without income restrictions.
   - **First Step**: Contribute the maximum allowable nondeductible amount ($7,500 or $8,600 if 50+) to a traditional IRA.
