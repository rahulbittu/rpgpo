## Finding 1: HSA vs FSA Comparison for 2026

**Key Differences:**

HSAs (Health Savings Accounts) and FSAs (Flexible Spending Accounts) serve different purposes in 2026:

- **HSA contribution limits for 2026:** $4,150 for self-only coverage and $8,300 for family coverage[1]
- **FSA contribution limits for 2026:** $3,300 maximum annual election[1]
- **HSA portability:** Funds roll over year-to-year and remain yours if you change jobs or retire[1]
- **FSA portability:** Subject to "use-it-or-lose-it" rules; unused funds typically don't carry over[1]
- **HSA investment options:** Can invest funds in stocks, bonds, and mutual funds for long-term growth[1]
- **FSA investment options:** Limited to spending on eligible expenses in the current plan year[1]

**Strategic consideration:** HSAs are better for long-term wealth building if you can afford high-deductible health plans (HDHPs). FSAs are better for predictable near-term medical expenses.

Source: https://www.healthcare.gov/glossary/health-savings-account-hsa/

## Finding 2: In-Network Provider Evaluation During Open Enrollment

**Steps to evaluate provider networks:**

1. **Verify your doctor's participation:** Use the plan's provider directory to search by name, specialty, or location before enrolling[2]
2. **Check specialist access:** Confirm that specialists you need (cardiologists, orthopedists, etc.) are in-network and accepting new patients[2]
3. **Evaluate hospital networks:** Ensure your preferred hospital system is included, especially for emergency care[2]
4. **Review telehealth options:** Many 2026 plans now include virtual care; verify coverage for your preferred telehealth providers[2]
5. **Confirm referral requirements:** Determine if your plan requires referrals to see specialists (common in HMOs)[2]

**Red flag:** Provider directories can be outdated. Call your doctor's office directly to confirm they accept the specific plan you're considering.

Source: https://www.healthcare.gov/choose-a-plan/

## Finding 3: Prescription Drug Tier Planning for 2026

**Understanding drug tiers:**

- **Tier 1 (Generic):** Lowest copay, typically $10–$15[3]
- **Tier 2 (Preferred Brand):** Mid-range copay, typically $30–$50[3]
- **Tier 3 (Non-Preferred Brand):** Higher copay, typically $50–$100[3]
- **Tier 4 (Specialty):** Highest copay or coinsurance, often 20–50% of drug cost[3]

**Action steps:**

1. **List your current medications** with dosages and frequency
2. **Check the formulary** (drug list) for each plan you're considering
3. **Verify tier placement** for your medications—the same drug may be on different tiers across plans
4. **Calculate annual costs:** Multiply monthly copays by 12 to compare total out-of-pocket costs
5. **Check for prior authorization requirements:** Some plans require approval before covering certain drugs[3]

**Example:** If you take a specialty medication costing $200/month on Tier 4 with 30% coinsurance, annual cost = $720. A different plan might place it on Tier 3 with a $75 copay = $900/year. The first plan saves you money despite higher coinsurance.

Source: https://www.medicare.gov/plan-compare/

## Finding 4: Dependent Coverage Considerations for 2026

**Key rules for dependents:**

- **Children up to age 26:** Can stay on parent's health plan regardless of marital status, student status, or financial dependence[4]
- **Spouse coverage:** Must be claimed as dependent; no age limit[4]
- **Domestic partners:** Coverage varies by plan and state; verify with your specific plan[4]
- **Cost impact:** Adding a spouse typically costs 30–50% more than self-only coverage; adding children costs 15–25% more[4]

**Open enrollment actions:**

1. **Verify dependent eligibility:** Confirm birth dates, Social Security numbers, and relationship status
2. **Compare family vs. self-only plans:** Calculate whether family coverage is cheaper than individual plans for each dependent
3. **Review dependent coverage limits:** Some plans cap dependent coverage at age 19 (or 23 if in school) for non-ACA plans
4. **Check for dependent deductibles:** Some family plans have individual deductibles per dependent plus a family deductible[4]

**Example calculation:** Self-only plan = $400/month. Family plan = $1,100/month. If spouse's individual plan = $350/month, family plan saves $50/month ($600/year).

Source: https://www.healthcare.gov/glossary/dependent/

## Plan for Optimizing Health Insurance Selection

### Week 1: Analyze HSA vs. FSA Options
- **Objective:** Determine which account aligns with your financial and healthcare needs.
- **Steps:**
  1. **Review Contribution Limits:**
     - HSA: $4,150 (individual), $8,300 (family) [1].
     - FSA: $3,300 maximum [1].
  2. **Assess Portability and Flexibility:**
     - HSA funds roll over and are portable [1].
     - FSA funds are subject to "use-it-or-lose-it" rules [1].
  3. **Evaluate Investment Opportunities:**
     - HSAs offer investment options for long-term growth [1].
     - FSAs do not offer investment opportunities [1].
  4. **First Step:** Calculate expected healthcare expenses and assess whether a high-deductible plan (required for HSA) suits your budget.

### Week 2: Evaluate In-Network Providers
- **Objective:** Ensure your preferred healthcare providers are covered.
- **Steps:**
  1. **List Current Providers:** Identify all doctors, specialists, and facilities you frequent.
  2. **Check Network Status:** Use your insurer’s online tool to verify if these providers are in-network.
  3. **Consider Alternatives:** If key providers are out-of-network, consider plans that include them.
  4. **First Step:** Access your insurer's online directory to begin your search.

### Week 3: Plan for Prescription Drug Tiers
- **Objective:** Minimize out-of-pocket prescription costs.
- **Steps:**
  1. **Inventory Current Medications:** List all prescriptions you need regularly.
  2. **Review Drug Tiers:** Check which tier your medications fall under in potential plans.
  3. **Compare Costs:** Evaluate copays and out-of-pocket costs for each plan.
  4. **First Step:** Contact your current pharmacy for a list of your medications and their insurance codes.

### Week 4: Consider Dependent Coverage
- **Objective:** Ensure comprehensive coverage for all dependents.
- **Steps:**
  1. **Assess Needs:** Evaluate the healthcare needs of each dependent.
  2. **Compare Plan Options:** Look at coverage limits, deductibles, and premiums for family plans.
  3. **Evaluate Additional Benefits:** Consider dental, vision, and wellness programs.
  4. **First Step:** Gather health records and anticipated healthcare needs for each dependent.
