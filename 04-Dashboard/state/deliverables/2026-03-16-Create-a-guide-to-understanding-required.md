# Required Minimum Distributions (RMDs) Under SECURE 2.0: Current Rules, Strategies, and Penalty Avoidance

## Finding 1: RMD Starting Age Increased to 73 (with Future Increase to 75)

The **SECURE 2.0 Act permanently raised the RMD starting age to 73** for many retirees, with a further increase scheduled to **75 for future cohorts**[6]. This change from the previous age of 72 provides additional years of tax-deferred growth for account owners. The law also introduced reduced penalties for missed RMDs, though proactive planning remains critical[6].

## Finding 2: The 10-Year Rule for Non-Spouse Beneficiaries (Effective 2026)

**Most non-spouse beneficiaries must withdraw the entire inherited IRA within 10 years** following the original owner's death[1]. This is the core change from the original SECURE Act (2019) that dismantled the "stretch IRA" strategy. The critical distinction is whether the original owner had already started taking RMDs:

- **If the original owner died BEFORE their RMD start date:** Beneficiaries have flexibility—they can defer distributions until year 10, take yearly distributions, or skip years, as long as the account is fully emptied by the end of the 10-year period[2].

- **If the original owner died ON or AFTER their RMD start date:** Annual RMDs must begin the year after death and continue through year 9, with the remaining balance distributed by year 10[2].

This distinction has "enormous practical consequences" for tax planning[2].

## Finding 3: Spousal Beneficiaries Have Distinct Advantages

Surviving spouses have options unavailable to other beneficiaries:

- **Rollover into their own IRA:** This resets the distribution timeline entirely. Instead of facing a 10-year drawdown, the surviving spouse can defer distributions until they reach RMD age (currently 73 under SECURE 2.0)[2].

- **Treat the account as their own:** They can delay RMDs until their own RMD age and base future RMDs on their life expectancy[4].

- **Elect to use the Uniform Lifetime Table:** Under SECURE 2.0, a surviving spouse can elect to use the Uniform Lifetime Table (based on their age) rather than the Single Life Expectancy Table, which can reduce the required annual distribution amount[4].

For a surviving spouse significantly younger than the deceased, a rollover can extend tax-deferred growth by years or even decades[2].

## Finding 4: Eligible Designated Beneficiaries (Exceptions to the 10-Year Rule)

Certain beneficiaries qualify for **life expectancy-based annual distributions** instead of the 10-year rule:

- Minor children of the account owner (until reaching the age of majority)
- Disabled individuals
- Chronically ill individuals
- Beneficiaries not more than 10 years younger than the decedent[1][4]

These exceptions provide significantly more favorable tax deferral compared to the standard 10-year rule.

## Finding 5: Roth Accounts Have Different Rules

**Roth 401(k)s are now exempt from RMDs during the original owner's lifetime**, aligning them with Roth IRAs[6]. However, once inherited by a beneficiary, RMD rules generally apply:

- **Inherited Roth IRAs** fall under the same 10-year rule for most non-spouse beneficiaries[2].
- **Qualified distributions remain tax-free** if the original Roth was open for at least five years[2].
- **Spousal beneficiaries** often have more flexibility, including the option to roll the assets into their own Roth IRA, potentially extending the RMD-free period[6].

## Key Findings

1. **RMD Starting Age**: The SECURE 2.0 Act increased the RMD starting age to 73, with a future increase to 75. This allows for additional tax-deferred growth.
2. **10-Year Rule for Non-Spouse Beneficiaries**: Non-spouse beneficiaries must withdraw inherited IRAs within 10 years of the original owner's death, with specific rules based on whether the original owner had started RMDs.
3. **Qualified Charitable Distributions (QCDs)**: QCDs can be used to satisfy RMDs while offering tax benefits.
4. **Penalty Avoidance**: The SECURE 2.0 Act reduced penalties for missed RMDs, but planning is crucial to avoid them.

## Detailed Analysis

### RMD Calculation for Traditional IRAs and 401(k)s

- **RMD Formula**: The RMD is calculated by dividing the account balance as of December 31 of the previous year by a life expectancy factor from the IRS Uniform Lifetime Table.
- **Example Calculation**: If an IRA balance is $500,000 and the life expectancy factor is 25.6, the RMD would be $500,000 / 25.6 = $19,531.25.

### Inherited IRA Rules Under SECURE Act 2.0

- **Pre-RMD Start Date**: Beneficiaries can defer distributions but must empty the account by the end of the 10th year.
- **Post-RMD Start Date**: Beneficiaries must take annual RMDs starting the year after the original owner's death, with the account fully distributed by year 10.

### Qualified Charitable Distributions (QCDs)

- **Strategy**: Individuals aged 70½ or older can direct up to $100,000 annually to charities directly from their IRAs, which can satisfy RMDs and reduce taxable income.
- **Example**: If an RMD is $20,000, a QCD of $20,000 can fulfill the RMD requirement, reducing taxable income by the same amount.

### Penalty Avoidance

- **Reduced Penalties**: The penalty for failing to take an RMD has been reduced from 50% to 25%, and further to 10% if corrected in a timely manner.
- **Proactive Steps**: Regularly review RMD requirements and set reminders to ensure timely withdrawals.
