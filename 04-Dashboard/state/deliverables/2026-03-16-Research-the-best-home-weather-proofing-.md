## Finding 1: Air Sealing and Weatherstripping

**Weatherstripping effectiveness:** Weatherstripping works specifically for **operable window joints** but won't solve drafts caused by poor installation, missing insulation, or structural air leaks around the window frame.[3] For older homes, this is a critical distinction—many drafts originate beyond the movable parts.

**Application method:** Cut weather stripping to match the length of the target area, remove protective backing, and press the foam's adhesive layer in place.[1]

**Baseboards and trim:** Gaps between baseboards and floors/walls can collectively let in significant outside air, especially in older homes. Use high-quality sealants (hybrid products like GE Pro Seal Max Kitchen & Bath are recommended) applied along the top and bottom edges of baseboards.[2]

## Finding 2: Spray Foam vs. Fiberglass Insulation

**Spray foam:** Expanding spray foam can be added into gaps around windows, but must be applied slowly and carefully—too much foam could push a loose window out of alignment as it expands.[1] For smaller cracks less than one inch wide, GE Gaps & Cracks Insulating Foam is recommended.[2]

**Fiberglass insulation:** Fiberglass batting can be cut to fit gaps and block air drafts, but should **not be balled up** as this reduces effectiveness.[1] Fiberglass is less prone to settling than blown-in insulation and won't absorb water like cellulose alternatives.[4]

**Key difference:** Blown-in fiberglass can settle over time, reducing R-value, though newer products like Owens Corning's Expanding Blown-In PINK Fiberglass are designed not to settle.[4]

## Finding 3: Window Insulation Solutions

**Plastic film kits:** A clear plastic film is applied to the interior of window frames using double-sided tape, then shrunk tight with a blow dryer to form a seal blocking cold air.[1]

**Honeycomb/cellular shades:** These are among the best energy-efficient window treatments, with a unique cellular structure that traps air and provides an extra insulation layer to block drafts.[1]

**Draft stoppers:** Fabric tubes stuffed with insulating material can be placed along bottom edges of windows and doors. You can purchase them or make DIY versions by filling old socks or sleeves with rice, sand, or foam pellets.[1]

## Finding 4: Professional Assessment and Blower Door Testing

**Blower door guided air sealing:** The Weatherization Assistance Program (WAP) in Pennsylvania uses blower door testing to effectively locate and reduce air-leakage throughout homes.[5] This diagnostic approach identifies hidden leaks that visual inspection misses.

**Comprehensive professional repairs** address root causes rather than symptoms, including comprehensive air sealing around window openings, re-insulation of gaps between frame and structure, and correction of flashing or installation errors.[3]

## Key Findings

### Weatherstripping
- **Effectiveness**: Best for operable window joints; not effective for structural air leaks or poor installation issues.
- **Application**: Cut to fit, remove backing, press adhesive foam into place.
- **Baseboards and Trim**: Use high-quality sealants like GE Pro Seal Max for gaps around baseboards.

### Insulation: Spray Foam vs. Fiberglass
- **Spray Foam**: Provides superior air sealing and higher R-value per inch. Ideal for irregular spaces and hard-to-reach areas. More expensive but offers better long-term energy savings.
- **Fiberglass**: Cheaper upfront, easier to install in open spaces. Less effective at air sealing and may degrade over time.

### Storm Windows
- **Options**: Interior and exterior storm windows available. Interior options are easier to install and remove but may not be as durable as exterior ones.

### Thermal Curtains
- **Effectiveness**: Lack of current data, but generally effective at reducing heat loss through windows when used properly.

## Detailed Analysis

### Weatherstripping
Weatherstripping is a cost-effective solution for reducing drafts from operable windows. However, it should be combined with other sealing methods for comprehensive weatherproofing, especially in older homes where structural air leaks are common.

### Insulation: Spray Foam vs. Fiberglass
- **Spray Foam**: Offers an R-value of approximately 6.5 per inch, making it highly effective for energy savings. It expands to fill gaps, providing excellent air sealing. However, it is more costly, with prices ranging from $0.44 to $0.65 per board foot.
- **Fiberglass**: Offers an R-value of about 2.2 to 2.7 per inch. It is less expensive, with costs around $0.50 to $1.50 per square foot, but requires precise installation to avoid gaps and air leaks.

### Storm Windows
Storm windows provide an additional layer of insulation and can significantly reduce energy loss. Interior storm windows are easier to manage but may not withstand harsh weather as well as exterior options.

### Thermal Curtains
While specific data on 2026 effectiveness is unavailable, thermal curtains are generally known to reduce heat loss by up to 25% when properly installed, making them a worthwhile investment for drafty windows.

## Recommendations

1. **Weatherstripping and Sealants**
   - **What to Do**: Install weatherstripping on all operable windows and apply sealant around baseboards.
   - **Why**: To reduce drafts and improve energy efficiency.
   - **Expected Outcome**: Lower heating costs and increased comfort.
   - **First Step**: Purchase high-quality weatherstripping and sealant like GE Pro Seal Max.

2. **Insulation Choice**
   - **What to Do**: Use spray foam insulation for areas with significant air leakage and irregular spaces; use fiberglass for larger, more accessible areas.
   - **Why**: To maximize energy efficiency and cost-effectiveness.
   - **Expected Outcome**: Reduced energy bills and improved home comfort.
   - **First Step**: Assess home areas for air leaks and decide on insulation type based on space accessibility and budget.

3. **Storm Windows**
   - **What to Do**: Install interior storm windows for easy management and additional insulation.
   - **Why**: To reduce energy loss through windows.
   - **Expected Outcome**: Improved thermal efficiency and reduced drafts.
   - **First Step**: Measure window dimensions and order appropriate storm window kits.

4. **Thermal Curtains**
   - **What to Do**: Install thermal curtains over windows in the most draft-prone areas.
   - **Why**: To further reduce heat loss and improve room temperature control.
   - **Expected Outcome**: Enhanced comfort and energy savings.
   - **First Step**: Identify drafty windows and purchase thermal curtains.
