## Research Home Energy Audit Best Practices
## Insulation Assessment
- Use **DOE Home Energy Score** (scale 1-10) to evaluate insulation condition alongside heating systems, water heaters, windows, and roof; includes customized recommendations and estimated utility bills[3].
- Perform **blower door test** to measure air leakage, or justify omission; required in qualifying audits per Iowa Multifamily Energy Audit Program using four Home Performance Assessment criteria[1].
- Cost for Home Energy Score assessment: **$150-$300**[3].

## HVAC Efficiency Evaluation
- Assess condition of **heating systems** (e.g., heat pumps with SEER, HSPF, AFUE ratings; Energy Star certificates required for tax credits)[2][3].
- Qualifying audits must recommend **lifecycle cost-effective controls and management systems**; compare building energy use to similar buildings[1].
- For tax credits (Form 5695, 2025): Heat pumps/biomas equipment limited to **$2,000 annually**; must include efficiency ratings documentation[2].

## Appliance Energy Ratings
- Evaluate **water heaters** and other systems via DOE Home Energy Score for condition and efficiency recommendations[3].
- HVAC/appliances: Require **Energy Star certificates and efficiency ratings** (SEER, HSPF, AFUE) for credits; 2025 manufacturer QM codes and PINs mandatory[2].
- Lighting: Replace inefficient bulbs with **LED, CFL, or Halogen** in existing fixtures, identified in audit; must decrease energy consumption[1].

## Phantom Load Identification
- No direct mentions in recent sources; DOE Home Energy Score covers overall energy use including potential standby loads via utility bill estimates and low-cost tips, but lacks specific phantom load protocols[3].
- General audit standards (e.g., EA 2020 compliance) include onsite walkthroughs for comprehensive evaluation, potentially covering standby power[4].

## ROI Calculation
- Use **DOE’s Audit Template tool** for “Annual Energy & Costs Savings" and "Payback with Incentives" outputs; required for qualifying audits[1].
- Home Energy Score provides **estimated utility bills** and customized improvements to inform ROI via energy savings[3].
- Tax credits offset costs: **$150 annual limit** for certified audits (must include written recommendations, principal residence only)[2].

## General Standards and Tools
- **Certified energy auditor** required for audits qualifying for credits; must meet professional standards[2].
- **ENERGY Star Portfolio Manager (ESPM)** for reporting annual energy/water use; third-party verification every 5 years[6].
- **Four Home Performance Assessment criteria** (Iowa standard, dated 2026): Blower door, lifecycle recommendations, DOE template, peer comparison[1].
- Upcoming training: FEMP workshop on **facility site walks and energy audit pathways** (March 20, 2026) for EA 2020/EISA 432 compliance[4].

## Detailed Analysis

### Insulation Assessment
- **DOE Home Energy Score**: Provides a comprehensive evaluation of insulation, heating systems, and other components, offering customized recommendations.
- **Blower Door Test**: Essential for measuring air leakage, which impacts insulation effectiveness. This test is a standard requirement in many energy audits.

### HVAC Efficiency Evaluation
- **Efficiency Ratings**: Use SEER, HSPF, and AFUE ratings to determine the efficiency of heating systems. Energy Star certification is crucial for accessing tax credits.
- **Lifecycle Cost-Effective Controls**: Implement systems that optimize energy use, comparing current usage with similar buildings to identify improvement areas.

### Appliance Energy Ratings
- **Energy Star Certification**: Ensure appliances meet efficiency standards to qualify for tax benefits. This includes HVAC systems and lighting upgrades.

## Recommendations

1. **Conduct a Home Energy Score Assessment**:
   - **Why**: Provides a comprehensive overview of your home's energy efficiency.
   - **Expected Outcome**: Identify key areas for improvement and potential cost savings.
   - **First Step**: Schedule an assessment with a certified provider ($150-$300).

2. **Perform a Blower Door Test**:
   - **Why**: Essential for detecting air leaks that compromise insulation.
   - **Expected Outcome**: Improved insulation performance and reduced energy costs.
   - **First Step**: Hire a professional to conduct the test as part of the energy audit.

3. **Evaluate HVAC Systems**:
   - **Why**: Ensure systems are operating efficiently and qualify for tax credits.
   - **Expected Outcome**: Lower energy bills and potential tax savings.
   - **First Step**: Check current systems for Energy Star certification and efficiency ratings.

4. **Upgrade Lighting and Appliances**:
   - **Why**: Reduce energy consumption and qualify for tax credits.
   - **Expected Outcome**: Decreased utility bills and improved energy efficiency.
   - **First Step**: Replace old bulbs with LED, CFL, or Halogen alternatives.

5. **Develop a Phantom Load Checklist**:
   - **Why**: Identify and mitigate energy waste from devices on standby.
   - **Expected Outcome**: Further reduction in energy usage.
   - **First Step**: List common culprits (e.g., chargers, TVs) and unplug when not in use.

6. **Create a Standardized ROI Calculation Method**:
   - **Why**: Quantify the financial benefits of energy efficiency improvements.
   - **Expected Outcome**: Better decision-making for energy investments.
   - **First Step**: Research existing ROI models and adapt them to your specific needs.
