## Key Findings

### Claiming Age Strategies for Couples
- **Higher-Earning Spouse Delay**: Delaying the higher-earning spouse's benefits until age 70 can increase their benefit by up to 77% from age 62, or 24-32% from full retirement age (FRA). This also maximizes survivor benefits for the surviving spouse, who will receive the larger of the two benefits. [1][4][6]
- **Lower-Earning Spouse Early Claim**: The lower-earning spouse may claim benefits as early as age 62, receiving spousal benefits at 50% of the higher earner's FRA benefit. However, early claiming reduces this amount significantly, potentially costing couples $800-$1,200 monthly over 20 years. [2]

### Spousal Benefit Coordination
- **Strategic Coordination**: Have the lower earner claim spousal benefits early while the higher earner delays. This strategy uses the higher benefit as the "backbone" for lifetime and survivor protection, avoiding permanent reductions in spousal benefits if both claim early. [1][2]
- **Survivor Benefits**: Prioritize the higher earner's delayed amount to benefit the surviving spouse longest. For example, a husband claiming at 70 might receive ~$4,000/month versus $2,250 if claimed at 62, significantly impacting survivor income. [3][4]

### Break-Even Analysis
- **Focus on Survivor Income**: Break-even analysis should prioritize the survivor's income, ensuring the surviving spouse receives the maximum possible benefit over their lifetime.

### Impact of Continued Work
- **Earnings Test**: If either spouse continues to work while claiming benefits before reaching FRA, benefits may be reduced due to the earnings test. However, these reductions are recalculated at FRA, potentially increasing future benefits. [5]

### Integration with Pension Income
- **Pension Coordination**: Consider how pension income affects Social Security benefits, especially if the pension is from work not covered by Social Security. The Windfall Elimination Provision (WEP) and Government Pension Offset (GPO) can reduce Social Security benefits. [6]
