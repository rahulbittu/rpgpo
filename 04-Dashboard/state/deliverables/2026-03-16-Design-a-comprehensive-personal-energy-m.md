## Finding 1: MEQ (Morningness-Evenness Questionnaire) for Chronotype Assessment
- **Best Practice**: Start chronotype assessment by having individuals or teams complete the **MEQ** at project kickoffs or team beginnings to identify morningness-eveningness profiles, enabling discussions on energy, focus, and preferred hours.
- **Evidence**: MEQ scores determine peak vs. non-peak windows; creative tasks (AUT test) showed stronger output when timed to chronobiological peaks (synchrony effect).
- **Implementation**: Share profiles team-wide; appoint a leader to model benefits for adoption.
- **Date/Context**: Article from Wharton Knowledge, no specific publication date but references ongoing neuroscience research.

## Finding 4: Recovery Protocols via Light Exposure and Sleep Consistency
- **Best Practice**: Prioritize **morning natural light exposure** (10-15 minutes within first hour of waking) to set circadian rhythm; evening: dim lights, avoid screens 1 hour pre-bed, use blue-blockers/night mode if needed.
- **Energy Mapping Tie-In**: Addresses chronotype shifts (e.g., teens to later types); consistency in sleep-wake timing as critical as duration.
- **Numbers**: 50-fold individual sensitivity range to light's circadian effects (age/genetics/eyesight dependent); sun lumens dwarf artificial light.
- **Risks**: Late chronotype in adolescence links to depression/anxiety (PNAS review).
- **Date/Context**: Revolution Health Radio episode by Chris Kresser, recent podcast.
- Source: https://chriskresser.com/why-your-sleep-schedule-matters-as-much-as-duration/[4]
