## Best Practices for Building Executive Presence

Engineering directors build **executive presence** by communicating in business terms, increasing visibility in executive forums, and framing technical work as strategic impact. For remote teams, regular one-on-ones create space for engineers to flag issues like overload or disconnection, fostering trust equivalent to in-person interactions[1][2]. A podcast outlines a **three-part formula**: translate results into executive language linking to company goals, practice structured communication (clear, concise, aligned with priorities), and build relationships with senior leaders[4]. Specific to-do list for promotion: 1) Showcase strategic impact by connecting work to big-picture goals; 2) Share wins in meetings/emails with decision-makers; 3) Cultivate presence via confident communication; 4) Network with seniors; 5) Craft a consistent value story[4].

## Strategies for Managing Stakeholders

Propose prioritized bets with clear trade-offs, articulate what won't be done, and anchor in business outcomes to reduce executive ambiguity and demonstrate ownership[2]. In remote engineering, treat documentation as code—use ADRs, onboarding guides, runbooks—to capture knowledge lost in absent hallway talks, enabling stakeholder alignment without constant syncs[1]. Act on partial information to enable momentum, as inaction risks more than framed early decisions; CEOs value speed over perfection[2]. For engineering/manufacturing directors, use real workplace scenarios in training to tie skills like influencing across departments to measurable outcomes[3].

## Key Challenges

Technical leaders promoted for engineering excellence struggle with people skills: giving feedback, coaching underperformers, navigating conflict, and influencing stakeholders, leading to burnout and bottlenecks if they default to solo problem-solving[3]. Remote setups evaporate institutional knowledge from unwritten conversations, requiring deliberate documentation to avoid stakeholder misalignment[1]. Visibility gaps limit influence—invisible tech success erodes board trust, even with strong performance; directors must represent tech externally to partners/customers[2]. Vague feedback like "be more assertive" backfires, especially for introverts decoding what executives mean by presence[4].

## Key Findings

### Executive Presence
- **Communication in Business Terms**: Engineering directors should translate technical work into strategic business impact, aligning with company goals.
- **Visibility and Networking**: Increase presence in executive forums and network with senior leaders to showcase strategic contributions.
- **Structured Communication**: Use clear and concise communication aligned with company priorities to build credibility.
- **Remote Team Management**: Regular one-on-ones help address team issues like overload and disconnection, fostering trust.

### Stakeholder Management
- **Prioritized Bets**: Clearly articulate priorities and trade-offs to manage expectations and demonstrate strategic ownership.
- **Documentation as Code**: Use ADRs, onboarding guides, and runbooks to maintain knowledge continuity in remote settings.

## Recommendations

### Building Executive Presence

1. **Translate Technical Achievements**: Regularly present technical work in terms of its strategic value to the organization. This involves linking project outcomes to business objectives and using language that resonates with non-technical stakeholders.
   - **Expected Outcome**: Improved visibility and recognition among executive peers.
   - **First Step**: Identify key business goals and map current projects to these objectives.

2. **Engage in Executive Forums**: Actively participate in executive meetings and forums to increase visibility and influence.
   - **Expected Outcome**: Enhanced reputation as a strategic leader.
   - **First Step**: Schedule attendance at the next executive meeting and prepare to discuss how your team’s work aligns with strategic goals.

3. **Develop Structured Communication**: Practice delivering messages that are clear, concise, and aligned with organizational priorities.
   - **Expected Outcome**: Increased clarity and impact of communication with stakeholders.
   - **First Step**: Enroll in a communication workshop focused on executive-level presentations.

### Managing Stakeholders

1. **Propose and Prioritize Strategic Initiatives**: Clearly outline the strategic initiatives your team will focus on, including trade-offs and what will not be prioritized.
   - **Expected Outcome**: Reduced ambiguity and increased stakeholder buy-in.
   - **First Step**: Draft a list of current projects with proposed priorities and trade-offs.

2. **Enhance Documentation Practices**: Treat documentation as an integral part of the engineering process, ensuring it is as robust and accessible as code.
   - **Expected Outcome**: Improved knowledge sharing and continuity, especially in remote environments.
   - **First Step**: Implement a system for maintaining ADRs and runbooks.

## Action Items

1. **Map Projects to Business Goals** (1 week)
   - Review current projects and align them with the company’s strategic objectives.
   
2. **Prepare for Executive Forums** (2 weeks)
   - Identify upcoming executive meetings and prepare a presentation that highlights your team’s strategic contributions.

3. **Enroll in Communication Workshop** (1 month)
   - Find and enroll in a workshop or course that focuses on executive communication skills.

4. **Draft Strategic Initiative Priorities** (2 weeks)
   - Create a prioritized list of initiatives with clear trade-offs and share it with stakeholders for feedback.

5. **Implement Documentation System** (1 month)
   - Set up a process for creating and maintaining ADRs and runbooks, ensuring all team members contribute.
