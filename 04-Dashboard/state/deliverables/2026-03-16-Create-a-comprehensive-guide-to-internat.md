## Research Tax Treaty Implications
## Finding 1: US Primary Residence Exclusion for Foreign Real Estate Gains
US investors can exclude up to **$250,000** of capital gain tax-free on foreign property sales if it qualifies as their primary residence under Section 121, or **$500,000** for married couples filing jointly; this applies to sales in 2026 filed in 2027.[1]  
**Source:** https://www.taxesforexpats.com/articles/real-estate/capital-gains-tax-on-foreign-property.html

## Finding 2: Tax Treaties Confirm Source Country Taxation on Real Estate Gains with US Saving Clause
US tax treaties (e.g., via IRS Publication 901) often allow the property's location country to tax real estate gains under Article 13, but US "saving clauses" preserve US taxation rights for citizens/residents, limiting relief to Foreign Tax Credit via **Form 1116** rather than full exemption.[1]  
States typically ignore treaties, taxing gains independently.[1]  
File **Form 8833** to disclose treaty positions overriding US rules; always report gains in USD.[1]  
**Source:** https://www.taxesforexpats.com/articles/real-estate/capital-gains-tax-on-foreign-property.html

## Finding 3: Foreign Tax Credit for Double Taxation Relief
US investors pay US capital gains tax on foreign real estate profits but claim credits for foreign taxes paid using **Form 1116**, directly offsetting US liability dollar-for-dollar up to US tax due.[1]  
**Source:** https://www.taxesforexpats.com/articles/real-estate/capital-gains-tax-on-foreign-property.html

## Finding 4: 1031 Like-Kind Exchanges Limited for Foreign Real Estate
Foreign-to-foreign real property swaps may qualify for 1031 deferral if both are investment/business-use, but US-to-foreign or personal homes do not; updated rules apply to 2026 sales.[1]  
**Source:** https://www.taxesforexpats.com/articles/real-estate/capital-gains-tax-on-foreign-property.html

IRS memorandum (issued Feb 2026) clarifies treaty benefits under Sec. 884(e) for reverse foreign hybrids facing branch profits tax (BPT), requiring "qualified resident" status via beneficial ownership or publicly traded tests; aligns with 2016 US Model Treaty Article 1(6) fiscally transparent entity (FTE) rules, extending to 1996/2006 models and 1981 draft.[2]
