## State Tax Deductions
State tax deductions for 529 contributions vary by state and are not uniform with federal changes. Georgia provides up to **$8,000** state tax deduction per beneficiary for married joint filers. South Carolina allows 100% deductibility from state taxable income, up to the lifetime contribution limit for the account. Confirm state-specific rules, as they may not automatically align with federal expansions like OBBBA.[6]

## Investment Options
529 plans feature tax-deferred growth and tax-free distributions for qualified expenses, with options varying by state-sponsored plan. Compare plans across states for investment options, fees, and flexibility; no universal federal investment menu exists, but states offer age-based portfolios, static options, or custom choices. Expanded qualified expenses under OBBBA (effective 2026) include K-12 materials and post-secondary credentials, increasing plan utility.[5]

## Superfunding Strategies
For 2026, contribute up to **$19,000** per beneficiary (**$38,000** for married couples filing jointly) annually without using lifetime gift tax exemption, via 5-year gift tax averaging (front-loading $95,000/$190,000 over 5 years). Prioritize after securing retirement; target based on years to use and K-12 needs.[5]

## Rules for Grandparent-Owned Plans
Grandparents can own 529 plans, naming a grandchild as beneficiary; funds preserve tax benefits across generations, but large accounts may trigger generation-skipping taxes. Change beneficiary to another family member if unused; no mandatory withdrawal age. Roth IRA rollovers (up to **$35,000** lifetime per beneficiary) require 15+ years account age and apply annual limits (**$7,500** in 2026, **$8,600** age 50+).[3][4][7]
