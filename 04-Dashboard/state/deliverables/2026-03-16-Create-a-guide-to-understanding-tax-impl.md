## Finding 1: Form 1099-DA Implementation and Cost Basis Reporting Timeline

**Form 1099-DA** is now the primary IRS reporting mechanism for cryptocurrency transactions. Brokers must report **gross proceeds from 2025 sales on forms issued in 2026**, while **cost basis reporting begins in 2027 for transactions executed in 2026**[3]. This two-phase rollout means investors currently receive gross proceeds only, with detailed cost basis information (purchase price, acquisition date, and holding period) arriving next year[1].

The IRS has expanded **data matching capabilities**, allowing broker-reported crypto sales to be compared directly against amounts reported on Form 1040 and Form 8949[3]. This eliminates the previous era of anonymous cryptocurrency trading.

## Finding 2: Taxable Events and Transaction Classification

Cryptocurrency is classified as **property, not currency**, triggering taxable events across multiple scenarios[4]:

- **Selling crypto for fiat currency** (USD, EUR, etc.)
- **Trading one crypto for another** (e.g., ETH for BTC)
- **Using crypto to purchase goods or services** (taxed at fair market value)
- **Receiving staking rewards** (classified as ordinary income at receipt)
- **Receiving mining rewards** (ordinary income)
- **Receiving airdrops** (generally ordinary income at receipt)
- **Receiving tokens from hard forks** (taxable only if followed by an airdrop)[4]

## Finding 3: Capital Gains Tax Rates and Holding Period Rules

**Short-term capital gains** (held ≤ 1 year) are taxed as ordinary income at rates between **10% to 37%** depending on your tax bracket[2].

**Long-term capital gains** (held > 1 year) receive preferential federal rates:

**California residents face significantly higher rates**: California taxes all capital gains—including crypto—as ordinary income at rates up to **13.3%**, combined with the federal 20% long-term rate and 3.8% Net Investment Income Tax, resulting in marginal rates approaching **40% on crypto gains**[1].

## Analysis

### Cost Basis Tracking
- **Form 1099-DA**: Starting in 2027, brokers will report cost basis information for cryptocurrency transactions executed in 2026. This includes purchase price, acquisition date, and holding period, which are crucial for calculating capital gains or losses[1].
- **Current Situation**: Until 2027, investors must manually track their cost basis using transaction records. This includes keeping detailed records of all trades, purchases, and sales to accurately report on Form 8949.

### DeFi Yield Farming Taxation
- **Yield Farming**: Income generated from DeFi yield farming is considered taxable. It is typically classified as ordinary income at the time of receipt. This means that any tokens or rewards received through yield farming must be reported as income based on their fair market value at the time they are received[4].

### NFT Capital Gains
- **NFT Transactions**: Buying and selling NFTs are treated as capital transactions. The difference between the purchase price and the sale price determines the capital gain or loss. For example, if an NFT is purchased for $1,000 and sold for $1,500, the $500 gain is subject to capital gains tax[4].

### Staking Rewards Classification
- **Staking Rewards**: Classified as ordinary income when received. The fair market value of the tokens at the time they are received must be reported as income. For instance, if you receive staking rewards worth $200, this amount is added to your taxable income for the year[4].

### IRS Reporting Requirements
- **Form 1040 and Form 8949**: Investors must report cryptocurrency transactions on these forms. The IRS has enhanced its data matching capabilities to ensure reported figures match broker-reported data on Form 1099-DA[3].
- **Taxable Events**: Selling, trading, or using cryptocurrency triggers taxable events, which must be reported accurately to avoid penalties[4].

## Comparison

- **Cost Basis vs. Gross Proceeds**: Currently, only gross proceeds are reported, which can lead to discrepancies if cost basis isn't tracked accurately by the investor. From 2027, cost basis reporting will provide a more accurate tax liability calculation.
- **Ordinary Income vs. Capital Gains**: Staking and mining rewards are taxed as ordinary income, potentially leading to higher tax rates compared to capital gains on long-term holdings.
