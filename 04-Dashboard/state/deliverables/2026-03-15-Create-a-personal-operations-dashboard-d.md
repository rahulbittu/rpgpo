## Key Design Principles for Personal Dashboards

**Limit KPIs to 5-7 metrics per view** to avoid cognitive overload[3]. George Miller's research shows working memory handles 7 plus or minus 2 items simultaneously[3]. For personal dashboards, this means focusing on your highest-impact goals rather than tracking everything.

**Design around decisions, not data**[1]. Each metric should trigger an action—if a goal metric doesn't influence a decision you'll make, it doesn't belong on your dashboard[3]. For example, a fitness dashboard should show metrics that determine whether you adjust your workout routine, not just vanity metrics.

**Use visual hierarchy strategically**[3]. Place critical KPIs in the top-left quadrant where users naturally look first[3]. For a personal dashboard, your primary goal (e.g., net worth, weight, project completion %) should occupy this prime real estate.

**Set thresholds for intervention**[1]. Define specific boundaries that trigger action—for example, a budget alert when spending exceeds 80% of monthly allocation, or a habit streak alert when you miss 2 consecutive days[1].

**Enable role-based customization**[2]. Different life areas need different views: a financial view showing net worth and savings rate; a health view showing exercise frequency and sleep; a projects view showing completion percentage and milestones[2].

**Include context with every metric**[5]. A number without comparison is meaningless[5]. Track metrics against previous periods, targets, or benchmarks—for example, "Weight: 180 lbs (down 2 lbs from last week, target 175 lbs)."

**Use progressive disclosure**[3]. Start with summary cards showing overall status, then allow drill-down into details[3]. A personal dashboard might show "3 of 5 goals on track" at the top, with expandable sections for each goal.

## Detailed Analysis

### Layout Suggestions
- **Top-Left Quadrant**: Reserve this space for your most critical KPI. For example, if financial growth is a priority, display your net worth or investment returns here.
- **Central Area**: Use this space for secondary but still important metrics such as monthly savings rate, current project progress, or health metrics like weekly exercise hours.
- **Bottom Area**: Display less critical, more detailed information that supports the main KPIs, such as breakdowns of spending categories or detailed habit tracking.

### Feature Recommendations
