## Week 1: Building Emotional Awareness & Mental Clarity (Days 1-7)

Focus on **observation without judgment** to clear mental clutter[1].

- **Days 1-2:** Write about your current emotional state using specific, precise language. Replace vague terms like "bad" or "good" with exact descriptors such as "restless," "content," "irritated," or "hopeful"[1].
- **Days 3-4:** Track your emotions at three different points during the day. Note external and internal circumstances at each moment[1].
- **Days 5-6:** Review your entries and identify patterns. Which situations consistently trigger specific feelings?[1]
- **Day 7:** Rest day. Write briefly and unstructured if desired[1].

**Mental clarity benefit:** Externalizing worries stops them from looping in your mind, and identifying patterns creates distance between yourself and emotions through **cognitive defusion**—observing thoughts rather than getting trapped inside them[2].

- **Days 11-12:** Practice gratitude prompts: "Why am I grateful for these two small things?" and "When did I feel most like myself today?"[2]

# Metrics and Methods to Track Cognitive Performance Over 30 Days

Based on current research, **eye-tracking-based cognitive assessment (ETCA)** and **wearable sensor integration with AI modeling** represent the most validated approaches for continuous cognitive monitoring, though traditional neuropsychological batteries remain the gold standard for clinical settings.

## Primary Tracking Methods

### Eye-Tracking-Based Assessment (ETCA)
**ETCA measures cognitive function by tracking viewing time spent on correct answers during task completion**, completing full assessments in approximately 3 minutes[2]. The method evaluates four cognitive subdomains: delayed recall, judgment, working memory, and visuospatial function[2]. Research demonstrates ETCA scores correlate well with traditional neuropsychological tests and can detect cognitive decline with high accuracy[2]. Notably, ETCA's continuous metric (percentage of viewing time) is less prone to ceiling effects than binary scoring, capturing subtle performance variations across the 30-day period[2].

A 2026 study found that integrated eye-tracking markers significantly outperformed individual plasma biomarkers in discriminating cognitive impairment, with superior sensitivity (AUC: 0.865 vs. 0.699–0.826)[3].

### Wearable Sensor + AI Modeling
**Continuous wearable data combined with AI prediction models can forecast cognitive outcomes from passively collected behavioral and physiological data**[5]. A 10-month study of 82 cognitively healthy adults using continuously worn sensors identified the most predictive metrics for cognitive performance: sleep quality, heart rate, and atmospheric pollution exposure[5]. Environmental factors (weather, air quality) and physiological markers proved most predictive overall[5].

### Kpmyhr Framework (Multidimensional Assessment)
The **Kpmyhr system synthesizes multiple neurocognitive indicators into a unified metric** designed for high-stakes performance monitoring[1]. Core components include:

- **Attentional Resilience Index (ARI):** Uses pupillometry and eye-tracking to detect micro-pauses in concentration[1]
- **Cognitive Load Monitor (CLM):** Analyzes real-time working memory and problem-solving efficiency via neural effort markers[1]
- **Response Acceleration Profile (RAP):** Measures reaction speed and decision-making latency under time pressure, identifying performance degradation thresholds[1]
- **Emotional Stability Quotient (ESQ):** Assesses emotional modulation during high-stress tasks[1]

Kpmyhr has demonstrated high inter-rater reliability and predictive accuracy across diverse populations, distinguishing between transient fatigue and enduring cognitive decline better than conventional assessments[1].

## 30-Day Tracking Protocol

**Week 1-2:** Establish baseline using ETCA (3-minute assessment) or wearable baseline metrics. Track sleep, heart rate variability, and pollution exposure daily.

**Week 3-4:** Repeat ETCA assessment mid-period. Continue wearable monitoring. Identify performance trends relative to environmental factors (pollution, weather changes).

## Safety Considerations

**Eye-Tracking Limitations:** Requires 40 cm distance from monitor in a quiet room with proper lighting[3]. Not suitable for individuals with significant vision impairment or oculomotor disorders.

**Wearable Data Privacy:** Continuous physiological monitoring raises data security concerns—ensure encryption and HIPAA compliance if health data is involved[5].

**Ceiling/Floor Effects:** Self-reported cognitive outcomes may be less reliable than performance-based measures due to contextual bias, though self-reported measures showed higher predictability in the wearable study[5].

**Validation Gaps:** While ETCA shows clinical validity for dementia and mild cognitive impairment detection, its predictive accuracy for subtle cognitive changes in cognitively healthy individuals over 30 days requires further investigation[2].

**Plasma Biomarker Comparison:** Eye-tracking markers outperform blood biomarkers for sensitivity, but combining both modalities may provide complementary insights[3].
