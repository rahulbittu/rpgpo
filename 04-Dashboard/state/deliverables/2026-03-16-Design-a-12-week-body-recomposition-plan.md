## Nutrition Macros for Skinny-Fat Body Recomposition
Moderate calorie deficit combined with high protein intake supports fat loss while preserving muscle in skinny-fat individuals. Protein at **1.2–2.0 g per kg body weight daily** is recommended for those doing resistance training, avoiding very low-energy diets under 800 kcal/day without supervision.[1]

- Calorie cycling: Surplus on heavy lift days for muscle growth, deficit on rest days, maintenance overall to enable recomposition without scale weight change.[2]
- Focus: Prioritize protein for muscle protein synthesis alongside deficit; no specific macro splits like carb/fat percentages detailed, but adequate calories prevent under-eating pitfalls common in skinny-fat fixes.[2][3]

## Progressive Resistance Training Protocols
**Progressive overload** via increasing reps (e.g., from 5 to 6-8, avoiding 10-11 reps entering endurance range), longer static holds, or more weight builds muscle density during recomposition. Train muscle-strengthening activities **at least 2 days/week** per UK Chief Medical Officers' guidelines.[1]

- Sample skinny-guy program (Workout A Push Focus): Bench Press 3x5-8, Overhead Press 3x8-12, Squats 3x5-8, Dips 2x10-15, Triceps Pushdowns 2x12-15.[4]
- Sample (Workout B Pull Focus): Deadlifts 3x5-6, Pull-ups/Lat Pulldowns 3x8-12, Bent-Over Rows 3x8-12, Bicep Curls 2x12-15, Face Pulls 2x15-20.[4]
- Stages for skinny-fat: Cut first → find maintenance calories → recomp with progressive overload; avoid bulking initially to prevent added fat.[3] (Video dated Feb 20, 2026)[3]

## Key Findings
1. **Nutrition Macros**: A moderate calorie deficit with high protein intake (1.2–2.0 g per kg body weight) is crucial for muscle preservation and fat loss. Calorie cycling can be beneficial, with a surplus on heavy lift days and a deficit on rest days.
2. **Resistance Training**: Progressive overload is essential, focusing on increasing reps, weight, or static holds. Training should occur at least twice a week, with specific routines for push and pull exercises.
3. **Program Structure**: The plan should include distinct phases—initial cutting to reduce fat, followed by maintenance and recomposition through consistent training and nutrition adjustments.
4. **Safety and Monitoring**: Avoid very low-energy diets without supervision and ensure gradual progression to prevent injury. Regular tracking of progress through metrics like body measurements and strength gains is recommended.

## Detailed Analysis
### Nutrition Macros
- **Protein Intake**: 1.2–2.0 g/kg body weight daily to support muscle synthesis.
- **Calorie Cycling**: Implement a surplus on training days to fuel muscle growth and a deficit on rest days to facilitate fat loss. This approach helps maintain overall calorie balance conducive to recomposition.

### Progressive Resistance Training
- **Workout A (Push Focus)**:
  - Bench Press: 3 sets of 5-8 reps
  - Overhead Press: 3 sets of 8-12 reps
  - Squats: 3 sets of 5-8 reps
  - Dips: 2 sets of 10-15 reps
  - Triceps Pushdowns: 2 sets of 12-15 reps

- **Workout B (Pull Focus)**:
  - Deadlifts: 3 sets of 5-6 reps
  - Pull-ups/Lat Pulldowns: 3 sets of 8-12 reps
  - Bent-Over Rows: 3 sets of 8-12 reps
  - Bicep Curls: 2 sets of 12-15 reps
  - Face Pulls: 2 sets of 15-20 reps

### Progress Tracking Metrics
- **Body Measurements**: Track waist, chest, and limb circumferences bi-weekly.
- **Strength Gains**: Record weights lifted and reps achieved each session.
- **Visual Progress**: Take progress photos monthly for visual comparison.
- **Energy Levels and Recovery**: Monitor subjective energy levels and recovery times to adjust nutrition and rest as needed.

1. **Establish Baseline**: Calculate current calorie intake and protein needs based on body weight. First step: Use an online calculator to estimate maintenance calories.
2. **Implement Calorie Cycling**: Plan meals to ensure a calorie surplus on training days and a deficit on rest days. First step: Meal prep for the week, focusing on protein-rich foods.
3. **Start Resistance Training**: Begin with the outlined push and pull routines, ensuring proper form and gradual progression. First step: Schedule workouts at least twice a week, alternating between push and pull.
4. **Track and Adjust**: Use a journal or app to record body measurements, workout progress, and dietary intake. First step: Set up a tracking system before starting the program.
5. **Safety First**: Avoid drastic calorie cuts and ensure proper warm-up and cool-down to prevent injury. First step: Consult a healthcare provider or nutritionist if considering significant dietary changes.
