# Skip-Level Meeting Best Practices for Engineering Directors

Based on the search results provided, here are the key frameworks and actionable practices for conducting effective skip-level meetings:

## Core Purpose and Structure

**Skip-level meetings are direct communication channels between senior leaders and employees that bypass traditional hierarchies.**[4] These meetings typically last **30 to 60 minutes**[2] and serve multiple purposes: gathering feedback, understanding employee experiences, building trust, and identifying organizational insights that wouldn't surface through standard reporting lines.

## Pre-Meeting Preparation Framework

As a director preparing to conduct skip-level meetings, focus on these specific steps:

**Clarify Meeting Objectives**[1] — Determine whether the meeting is a general check-in, project-specific discussion, or feedback-gathering session. This clarity helps both parties prepare effectively.

**Research the Employee**[2] — Just as employees should research you, review their background, recent projects, performance, and career trajectory. This establishes rapport and demonstrates genuine interest.

**Communicate Transparently with Their Manager**[1] — Inform the direct supervisor about the upcoming meeting, agenda items, and goals. This ensures alignment and prevents perception of circumventing the management chain.

**Prepare Targeted Questions**[1] — Have specific questions ready that elicit meaningful feedback:
- How do you feel about the current direction of the team or project?
- What challenges are you facing that could be addressed more effectively?
- How can leadership better support you in achieving your goals?
- Are there processes or tools that could enhance team efficiency?

## During the Meeting

**Maintain Professional Openness**[1] — Approach with a professional demeanor, be honest and constructive, and avoid venting frustrations without offering potential solutions.

**Facilitate Two-Way Dialogue**[7] — Sharing insights should be bidirectional. As a director, update employees on cross-functional initiatives, business strategy, product changes, and performance metrics they may not have access to. Encourage them to share prospect feedback, day-to-day challenges, and progress updates.

**Encourage Participation**[5] — Integrate interactive elements rather than delivering one-sided information. Ask questions, invite input, and create space for employees to contribute ideas.

## Post-Meeting Actions

**Document Feedback and Action Items**[1] — Capture key discussion points and create a visual map of feedback and commitments. Use project management tools to track ownership and deadlines.

**Develop a Clear Action Plan**[1] — Assign specific responsibilities with deadlines for addressing feedback received. Maintain accountability through visible tracking.

**Follow Up with Transparency**[8] — Communicate outcomes broadly. When you implement changes based on skip-level feedback, share early wins and explain how employee input drove decisions. If roadblocks emerge, openly describe what happened and how you're adjusting.

## Critical Success Factors

**Set Clear Goals Hierarchically**[5] — Identify three main objectives for the meeting in priority order: one primary goal and two supporting goals. Communicate these clearly so employees understand the meeting's value.

**End Strongly**[5] — Use the final five minutes to push for actionable next steps and reinforce how the discussion connects to broader organizational goals.
