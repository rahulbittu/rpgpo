# 401(k) to Roth IRA Conversion Strategies for High Earners (2026)

## Key Rule Change: Mandatory Roth Catch-Up Contributions

Starting in 2026, **high earners making catch-up contributions to a 401(k) must direct them to a Roth 401(k)**, not traditional accounts[2]. If your plan lacks a Roth 401(k) option, you cannot make catch-up contributions at all, though alternative strategies exist[2][6].

## Primary Conversion Strategies

### 1. **In-Plan Roth Conversions (Direct)**

Many employer 401(k) plans now offer **in-plan Roth conversions**, allowing you to convert directly from your 401(k) to a Roth 401(k) without leaving the plan[1]. This approach:
- Processes faster than rollover conversions
- Maintains employer plan protections
- Avoids the pro-rata rule complications that plague IRA conversions[1]

**Important caveat:** In-plan conversions do not satisfy the mandatory Roth catch-up requirement if the conversion amount includes non-elective deferrals[6].

### 2. **Rollover Conversion (Two-Step)**

If your plan doesn't offer in-plan conversions, you must:
1. Roll your 401(k) balance to a traditional IRA
2. Convert that IRA to a Roth IRA[1]

This adds complexity but provides more investment flexibility[1].

### 3. **After-Tax Contributions + In-Plan Conversion (Mega Backdoor Roth)**

For high earners, this is the most powerful strategy[5]:
- The 2026 total 415(c) limit for 401(k) contributions (employer + employee combined) is **$72,000** ($80,000 if age 50+)[5]
- If your salary deferral is $24,500, you can potentially contribute an additional **$30,000–$40,000** in after-tax contributions annually[5]
- These after-tax contributions can be converted to a Roth 401(k) with minimal tax friction (only earnings are taxed)[3]
- Combined with standard backdoor Roth contributions ($8,600 for 2026, or $9,700 if age 50+), this creates a "massive tax-free engine"[5]

### 4. **Backdoor Roth IRA (for High Earners Above Income Limits)**

For 2026, Roth IRA income phaseouts begin at **$153,000** (single) or **$242,000** (married filing jointly)[3]. High earners can bypass this through:
1. Contribute $8,600 to a traditional IRA (non-deductible)
2. Convert to a Roth IRA[2]

**Critical caveat—The Pro-Rata Rule:** If you hold other pre-tax IRAs (traditional, SEP, or SIMPLE), the IRS requires you to convert a proportional share of all your IRA balances[1][5]. Example: If you have $100,000 in pre-tax IRAs and attempt to convert $8,600 in after-tax contributions, 91.4% of the conversion ($7,860) becomes taxable income[5].

## Timing Strategies to Minimize Tax Impact

**Multi-year conversions outperform lump-sum conversions[1]:**
- Convert during low-income years, not peak-earning years
- Spread conversions across multiple years to avoid bracket creep
- Monitor market timing: if your IRA drops from $100,000 to $92,000 between Monday and Wednesday, you owe taxes only on $92,000[1]

**Coordinate with major life events[4]:**
- Plan conversions around Social Security claiming (significant income change)
- Avoid years triggering Medicare surcharges (income thresholds affect IRMAA premiums)
- Consider years with temporarily reduced income

## Tax Consequences

Every dollar converted is added to your taxable income for that year[4]. However, Roth income:
- Does not negatively impact Social Security taxation
- Is not considered when determining Medicare premiums[3]
- Grows tax-free and requires no required minimum distributions (RMDs)[8]

## Critical Pre-Conversion Steps

1. **Calculate your total traditional IRA basis** across all accounts before any conversion[1]
2. **Contact your plan administrator** to confirm whether your 401(k) offers in-plan conversions or requires a rollover[1]
3. **Consult a tax professional** before executing backdoor conversions, especially if you hold existing pre-tax IRAs[2][5]
4. **Verify the five-year aging rule:** Converted balances must meet separate five-year aging rules to avoid penalties on withdrawals[2]
