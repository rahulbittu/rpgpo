## Weighted Matrix Analysis

Weighted matrix analysis, also known as a weighted decision matrix, evaluates options by assigning relative importance (weights) to criteria and scoring options against them. Multiply scores by weights, sum for totals, and select the highest scorer.

- **Step-by-step methodology (Creately, 2023)**: Assign weights on 1-5 scale (e.g., Cost=3, Ease of Use=5); score options 1-10; calculate weighted scores (score × weight); sum totals (e.g., Software A=112 wins).[1]
- **Example calculation (Business News Daily, undated)**: Criteria weights: Workload=3, Deadlines=2, Payroll=5. Scores yield Hire Employee=25 total vs. Don't Hire=31 total, favoring no hire due to payroll weight.[5]
- **Advanced variant: SWARA weighting (Khalilzadeh, 2026)**: Rank criteria by expert votes; compute relative differences (Sj), growth parameters (Kj=1+Sj), normalized weights via qj=Kj/ΣKj. Used in construction projects for objective prioritization without pairwise comparisons.[2]
- **WASPAS method (Khalilzadeh, 2026)**: Combines weighted sum (WSM) and product (WPM); normalize matrix, compute Qi = 0.5×(sum + product terms), rank descending. Higher accuracy than independents; steps include multiplicative nondimensional matrix.[2]
- **Tool with confidence score (Tool Strategy Hub, undated)**: Weights as multipliers (e.g., Cost×4, Scalability×5); scores 1-10; confidence = delta between #1 and #2 (e.g., 12% tie, 90% clear winner).[4]
