## Finding 1: Adults Need 7-9 Hours of Consistent, Uninterrupted Sleep; Maintain Fixed Schedules and Avoid Disruptors
Most adults require **7-8 hours** of sleep per night for recovery, with continuity key as fragmented sleep reduces quality; major scientific societies recommend fixed bed/wake times daily (within 1 hour), no late exercise/stimulating activities, limited evening screens (blue light blocks melatonin), and reduced caffeine/alcohol/heavy/spicy meals.[1][2][3]

## Finding 2: Follow the 10-5-3-2-1 Rule for Evening Wind-Down (2026 Guide)
Use this timed protocol: no caffeine **10 hours** before bed, no alcohol **5 hours** before, no heavy meals **3 hours** before, end work **2 hours** before, no screens **1 hour** before; pair with 10-20 minute rituals like gentle yoga, reading physical books, or to-do lists.[3] Optimal bedroom: **60-67°F**, dark (blackout curtains), quiet (white noise/fan), sleep/sex only.[3][4]  
**Source**: https://www.calm.com/blog/world-sleep-day[3]; https://www.sleepapnea.org/sleep-health/sleep-hygiene/[4]

## Finding 3: Daytime Exercise Improves Sleep Depth; Experiment with Timing and Intensity
Regular activity like brisk walking, strength training, or yoga enhances sleep onset/depth; higher-intensity aerobic (jogging, swimming) or resistance yields stronger effects than sedentary days—start small if inactive; avoid evenings if it disrupts melatonin, but test personal response as evidence varies.[2][4] Daytime stress relief (e.g., **5-minute breathing**) aids night sleep.[3]
