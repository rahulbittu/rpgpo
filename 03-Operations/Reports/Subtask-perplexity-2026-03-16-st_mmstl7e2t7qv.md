## Timing Strategies
- **Target Q3-Q4 budget cycles**: Start conversations 6-12 weeks before performance reviews, ideally September-November for January raises, as budgets are set then. Avoid December when funds are already allocated.[1]
- **Post-job offer is optimal**: Negotiate during the offer meeting after receiving the letter; express gratitude first, then justify with data. Early interview mentions risk seeming pay-focused.[2]
- **Recent example (Feb 23, 2026)**: Jessica McCauley advises timing negotiations around new job offers to align with industry standards researched via Levels.fyi, Indeed, Glassdoor.[3]

## Market Data Preparation
- **Use specific benchmarks**: Cite platforms like Levels.fyi, Indeed, Glassdoor for salary ranges; frame as "Based on market rates for this role and my achievements at [previous role], X is appropriate." Harvard research supports data-backed, collaborative framing over emotional asks.[1][3][4]
- **Justify with evidence**: Include specialized skills, certifications, experience reducing ramp-up, or competing offers. Example script: "Could we meet at 92? That's a fair reflection of my impact and the market."[1]
- **Full package view**: Research bonuses, PTO, development budgets, hybrid options; Harvard Business School professor notes negotiating total comp creates tradeoffs.[3][4]

## Handling Rejection
- **Propose alternatives immediately**: If budget-constrained, suggest performance bonuses, extra PTO, development budget, or 6-month revisit with milestones. Script: "I understand constraints. Could we explore a bonus tied to targets or timeline to that number in 6 months?"[1]
- **Follow up in writing**: Within 24 hours, email summary of discussion and agreements to lock in next steps like "revisit in 6 months with benchmarks."[1]
- **Stay collaborative long-term**: Avoid ultimatums; demonstrate professionalism per Harvard's Program on Negotiation experts Kurtzberg and Naquin.[4]
