# Tax-Loss Harvesting: Current Strategies, Rules, and Tax Bracket Impact

**Tax-loss harvesting is a strategy that allows investors to sell underperforming investments at a loss to offset capital gains and reduce taxable income, with potential year-round application rather than just at year-end.**[1][4]

## Core Mechanics

The strategy works by offsetting capital gains with capital losses realized in the same tax year.[2] If losses exceed gains, you can deduct up to **$3,000 per year against ordinary income**, with excess losses carrying forward to future tax years.[2] For example, a $10,000 long-term capital gain taxed at 20% ($2,000 liability) can be reduced to $4,000 taxable gain ($800 liability) by harvesting a $6,000 loss—saving $1,200 in taxes.[1]

## Wash Sale Rules: Critical Compliance

The **wash sale rule** prohibits claiming a loss if you purchase a "substantially identical" security within 30 days before or after the loss-sale date.[3] To maintain portfolio exposure while complying:

- Replace a losing technology stock with a broad technology ETF rather than the identical stock[1]
- Use the "doubling up" strategy: buy the identical position at current price, then wait 30+ days before selling the original loss position[3]
- Track the 30-day window carefully on both sides of the transaction[3]

## Tax Bracket Considerations

**Higher-income earners benefit more significantly from tax-loss harvesting.** The strategy is particularly valuable for investors in higher tax brackets because:

- Capital gains tax rates range from 0% to 20% depending on income level[1]
- Each dollar of loss offset saves proportionally more in higher brackets
- The strategy is especially effective for investors with high trading turnover and short-term capital gains[4]

For 2026, the long-term capital loss carryforward limit has increased to **$132,900**, allowing larger loss offsets compared to prior years.[2]

## Optimal Timing and Opportunities

Tax-loss harvesting is **not limited to year-end** and can be applied opportunistically throughout the year.[1][4] Best opportunities include:

- Periods of market volatility and sector rotation[1]
- After market corrections[1]
- During portfolio rebalancing[1]
- Even in up markets: year-to-date through August 2025, more than 400 stocks in the S&P 500 experienced at least a 5% drawdown[3]

## Implementation Best Practices

- **Track cost basis carefully** to identify harvestable losses[1]
- **Monitor realized and unrealized gains** to time harvesting strategically[1]
- **Evaluate transaction costs** to ensure tax savings exceed trading fees[1]
- **Maintain diversification** when replacing sold positions[1]
- **Align with long-term allocation** rather than selling purely for tax reasons[1]
