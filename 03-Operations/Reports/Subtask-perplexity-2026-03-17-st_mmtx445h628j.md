## Legality and Availability for High Earners in 2026
Backdoor Roth IRA conversions remain fully legal in 2026 for individuals earning over $200k, allowing indirect Roth contributions by bypassing direct income limits through nondeductible Traditional IRA contributions followed by conversion.[2][3] No federal legislation, including SECURE Act 2.0, prohibits this for 2026 tax year despite prior debates targeting incomes over $400k.[2]

## 2026 Income Limits Triggering Backdoor Need
Direct Roth IRA contributions phase out at MAGI of $153,000–$168,000 (single) or $242,000–$252,000 (married filing jointly), making backdoor essential for those over $200k.[2][3] Conversions have no income limits.[3]

## Contribution Limits
- Under age 50: $7,500 nondeductible Traditional IRA contribution, then convert to Roth.[2][3][7]
- Age 50+: $8,600 (includes $1,100 catch-up).[2][3][7]

## Step-by-Step Process and Timeline
1. **Contribute nondeductible funds to Traditional IRA**: Use after-tax dollars up to limit; can contribute for prior year (e.g., 2025 for 2024) by April 15, 2026. File Form 8606 if prior-year basis not already reported ($50 penalty if missed).[1][3]
2. **Convert to Roth IRA**: Do promptly (same day ideal) to minimize taxable earnings; no waiting period required but track basis.[3][4]
3. **Handle reporting**: Receive 1099-R in year after conversion (e.g., 2026 for 2025 conversion); report on taxes via two-step TurboTax process or Form 8606. If 2025 contribution for 2024, verify 2024 Form 8606 first.[1]
4. **Tax filing deadline**: By April 15 following conversion year (e.g., April 2027 for 2026).[1]

**Timeline example for 2026**:
- Jan 1–April 15, 2026: Contribute $7,500 nondeductible to Traditional IRA (for 2025 or 2026).
- Immediately after: Convert to Roth.
- 2027: File 2026 taxes with Form 8606 and 1099-R.[1][2]

## Pro-Rata Rule and Tax Calculations
IRS aggregates all Traditional/SEP/SIMPLE IRAs; taxable portion = (pre-tax IRA balance / total IRA value) x conversion amount at year-end.[2][4]  
**Example calculation**: $10k after-tax basis + $90k pre-tax = $100k total. Convert $7,500: Taxable = ($90k / $100k) x $7,500 = $6,750 at ordinary rates.[2][4]  
**Avoidance strategy**: Roll pre-tax IRAs to employer 401(k) first to clear aggregation, enabling tax-free conversion.[4]

## Strategies for High Earners Over $200k

- **Roll to 401(k)**: Move existing pre-tax IRAs to employer plan before contributing/converting.[4]
- **Mega Backdoor alternative**: If 401(k) allows after-tax contributions + in-plan Roth conversions, add up to $46,500 after-tax (2026 est. beyond $23,500 deferral), convert promptly; requires plan support and cash flow.[5]
