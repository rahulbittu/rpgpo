# Tax Optimization for W-2 Workers with Side LLC: 2026 Guide

## Finding 1: Self-Employment Tax Rate & Calculation

**Self-employment tax for LLC owners is 15.3%**, applied to 92.35% of net earnings (not the full amount, since the 7.65% employer-equivalent portion is excluded from the base)[1]. For example, on $100,000 in net profit, you'll owe approximately $14,130 in self-employment taxes[1].

**Calculation method:**
1. Calculate net business profit: Total revenue – Deductible business expenses
2. Multiply net profit by 92.35%, then multiply that result by 15.3%
3. Stack LLC profit on top of W-2 wages and other income
4. Apply federal (and state) income tax brackets to the combined total[2]

Your **effective tax rate** equals total tax divided by total income, which typically includes both income tax and self-employment tax[2].

## Finding 2: S Corporation Election for Self-Employment Tax Reduction

If your LLC generates **more than $60,000 in net profit annually**, electing S corporation status is one of the most effective ways to reduce self-employment tax[1]. By filing **IRS Form 2553**, you split income into two categories: a reasonable W-2 salary subject to payroll taxes, and distributions that are not[1].

**Real example:** On $120,000 in net profit, paying yourself a $60,000 salary and taking the rest as distributions could save approximately **$4,600 in self-employment taxes annually**[1].

**Critical deadline:** For the 2026 tax year, calendar-year businesses must file Form 2553 by **March 16, 2026**[1].

## Finding 3: Quarterly Estimated Tax Payments

If you expect to owe **more than $1,000 in tax for the year**, you generally need to pay quarterly estimated taxes to avoid penalties[3]. You can generally avoid penalties by paying at least:
- **90% of the current year's tax**, or
- **100% of the prior year's tax** (110% for higher earners) through estimates and/or withholding[3]

**For W-2 workers with LLC income:** You can cover your estimated tax liability from your LLC by either making quarterly payments **or** increasing the tax withholding from your W-2 job's paycheck[3].

## Finding 4: Deductible Business Expenses & Retirement Contributions

**Qualified Business Income (QBI) deduction (Section 199A):** Pass-through LLC owners can deduct **up to 20% of qualified business income**[3]. This is available regardless of whether you itemize deductions or take the standard deduction[6].

**Retirement contribution limits (2025-2026):**
- **Solo 401(k):** Total limit (employee + employer) is $70,000[3]
- **SEP IRA or SIMPLE IRA:** High contribution limits available[3]
- **401(k) elective deferral limit for 2026:** $24,500 (plus $8,000 catch-up for age 50+)[4]

**Other deductible expenses:**
- Charitable donations (by December 31)[6]
- Tax loss harvesting (selling investments at a loss to offset capital gains and up to $3,000 of ordinary income)[6]
- Consistent expense tracking throughout the year[2]

## Finding 5: W-2 + LLC Income Coordination Strategy

**Key coordination points:**
- Watch withholding and bonus timing on your W-2 job so you don't get caught with an unexpected balance due at tax season[4]
- Balance W-2 wages, LLC distributions, and reasonable compensation requirements if you elect S corp status[4]

## Finding 6: 2026 New Tax Deductions (Limited Applicability)

For 2025-2028 tax years, four new deductions were created, though most are employee-focused:
- **Tip income deduction:** Up to $25,000 of tips received at work
- **Overtime pay deduction:** Up to $12,500 (or $25,000 for married filing jointly)
- **Age 65+ deduction:** Up to $6,000 ($12,000 if married filing jointly)[5]

These are unlikely to apply to most side LLC owners unless you also receive W-2 tips or overtime.
