## Finding 1: HSA Triple Tax Advantage
HSAs provide a **triple tax advantage**: contributions are tax-deductible (reducing taxable income directly, even without itemizing), investment growth is tax-free, and withdrawals for qualified medical expenses are tax-free at any age.[1][2][4] Qualified expenses include doctor visits, hospital stays, mental health services, chiropractic care, medical equipment, and certain OTC medications, covering spouse and dependents.[1] After age 65, non-medical withdrawals avoid the 20% penalty but incur income tax, similar to a traditional IRA, with no required minimum distributions.[1][3]

## Finding 2: 2026 HSA Contribution Limits
**2026 individual limit**: $4,400; **family limit**: $8,750, plus $1,000 catch-up for age 55+.[1] One source mentions a $7,500 household limit starting 2026 under new rules, but this conflicts with primary IRS-aligned reports.[5] Eligibility requires a High-Deductible Health Plan (HDHP); Bronze plans qualify in 2026.[1] Contributions via payroll or personal are deductible; maxing yields significant tax savings (e.g., $4,000+ subsidy in 35-37% brackets).[3]

## Finding 3: Available Investment Options
HSA providers offer investments like **mutual funds, index funds, or other securities**, mirroring 401(k) options for tax-free growth.[1] Funds can grow long-term as a wealth-building tool beyond medical spending.[2]

## Finding 4: Strategies for HSA as Retirement Vehicle
- **Shoebox Strategy** (from Stephen Lee, CPA, Feb 19, 2026): Pay current medical expenses out-of-pocket, save receipts, invest HSA funds to grow (potentially to 7 figures), reimburse tax-free in retirement.[3]
- Delay reimbursements: Cover today's costs with cash flow, let HSA invest; reimburse later for expanded 2026 qualified expenses, turning it into a tax-free retirement reserve.[2][3]
- Max contributions annually, invest aggressively while eligible for HDHP, use post-65 flexibility for any withdrawals (taxed non-medically but penalty-free).[1][2]
- Integrate with financial planning to cut lifetime taxes; keep receipt backups for audits.[3]
