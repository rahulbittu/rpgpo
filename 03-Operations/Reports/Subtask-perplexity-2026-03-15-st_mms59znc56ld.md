## Finding 1: Tax Treatment of ISOs and NSOs for Startup Employees
Incentive Stock Options (ISOs) granted to employees incur no regular income tax at grant, vesting, or exercise (if holding requirements are met), but may trigger Alternative Minimum Tax (AMT) at exercise based on the spread between exercise price and fair market value (FMV); long-term capital gains apply on sale after holding periods. Non-Qualified Stock Options (NSOs), granted to employees or nonemployees, tax the spread at exercise as ordinary income (subject to payroll taxes and withholding), with post-exercise gains as capital gains. ISOs limit exercisable value to $100,000 per calendar year (based on FMV at grant) and must be exercised within 10 years (or 3 months post-termination).

## Finding 2: Section 83(b) Elections for Early Exercise of NSOs
For NSOs exercised early (before vesting), employees may file a Section 83(b) election within 30 days of exercise to treat the spread as ordinary income at exercise (based on low early FMV), potentially converting future appreciation to capital gains instead of ordinary income at vesting. This is optional for NSOs but not required. Startups must maintain current 409A valuations (refreshed annually or post-funding) to set exercise prices at or above FMV, avoiding Section 409A violations (immediate taxation + 20% penalty).

## Finding 3: AMT Impact on ISO Exercises
ISO exercises can trigger AMT for employees, calculated on the bargain element (FMV minus exercise price) added to alternative minimum taxable income; companies file Form 3921 for ISO exercises. Example: Reclassifying 5 contractors paid $100,000 each in 2025 as employees could owe $38,250 in back taxes, highlighting related compliance risks. All 2025 stock issuances, option grants, exercises, and 409A valuations must be documented by March 16, 2026, for tax prep.
