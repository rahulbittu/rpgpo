## Sprint Planning: Best Practices and Challenges

Sprint planning sessions last 1 hour per week of sprint length, including steps like reviewing objectives, discussing new info, confirming capacity and definition of "done," estimating work via story points or planning poker, assigning ownership, and achieving team consensus.[1][7] Sprints typically run 2-4 weeks to deliver focused chunks, with tools like Jira (used by 62% of companies per Digital.ai's 17th State of Agile survey) for backlog management and prioritization.[2][5] **Best practices** include AI-driven forecasting to predict 10% velocity drops based on throughput, adjusting plans empirically, and using affinity grouping for quick sizing of large backlogs during grooming.[4][7] **Challenges** involve unclear/changing requirements (top bottleneck at 35% of teams), hidden complexity revealed mid-sprint, and ensuring timely decisions in cross-functional teams.[6][5]

- **Tool examples**: monday.com (unlimited plan $7/user/month yearly), Jira, Miro, ClickUp, Scrum Mate ($29/month for 7 users, 15-day trial).[1]
- **Next steps**: Run planning poker on 5-10 backlog items using Jira; trial Scrum Mate for small teams under 7.

## Standups (Daily Scrums): Best Practices and Challenges

Daily scrum meetings last 15 minutes (implied standard), where teams account for prior day's actions, update needs, and refine plans to maintain focus on 1-4 week sprint chunks.[5] **Best practices** emphasize cross-functional collaboration (designers, engineers, researchers) for timely decisions, running UX sprints ahead of implementation to avoid delays.[5] Integrate with DevOps for CI/CD alignment, ensuring sprint commitments lead to shippable code.[4]

- **Challenges**: Finalizing research/decisions on time (e.g., feature categories for search screens); requirements changes slowing teams (35% cite as #1 issue).[5][6]
- **Next steps**: Limit standups to 3 questions (yesterday, today, blockers); log via Jira for 62% industry-standard tracking.[2]

## Retrospectives: Best Practices and Challenges

Retrospectives occur at sprint end (2-4 weeks) to log progress, review processes, and plan improvements for higher-quality output in less time.[2][5] **Best practices** leverage AI analytics for data-driven insights on risks/backlogs, augmenting Scrum Masters without replacement; focus on empirical leadership like velocity adjustments.[4] Bridge Agile-DevOps for faster market delivery with resilient pipelines.[4]

- **Challenges**: Rigid processes needing strong leadership; less documentation upfront in iterative methods like Scrum (teams of 5-9).[3]
- **Next steps**: Use AI dashboard (e.g., predict 10% story point shortfall); vote on 3 improvements per retro via Miro.[1][4]

## Velocity Tracking: Best Practices and Challenges

Velocity tracks story points completed per sprint (e.g., forecast 10% drops via ML on historical data), using relative estimation like T-shirt sizing or planning poker.[4][7] **Best practices** discuss concrete drivers (dependencies, QA at 16% bottleneck), re-vote if no convergence, signaling refinement needs; AI flags risks for prioritization.[4][7][6] Jira supports this for 62% of Agile teams.[2]

| Technique | Description | When to Use |
|-----------|-------------|-------------|
| Planning Poker | Team votes on complexity, discuss drivers like data availability | Individual items in planning[7] |
| Affinity Grouping | Sort backlog smallest-to-largest | Large sets in grooming/release planning[7] |
| AI Forecasting | Predicts velocity (e.g., -10% next sprint) | Ongoing retros/planning[4] |

- **Challenges**: Unclear specs double QA delays (35% vs 16%); fragile planning at AI speeds.[6][7]
- **Next steps**: Track in Jira weekly; refine top 35% bottleneck items pre-sprint.[2][6]
