## Finding 2: Action Item Structure and Accountability Metrics

The industry standard for effective action items follows the **S.M.A.R.T. goal format** (Specific, Measurable, Achievable, Relevant, Time-bound)[2]. Action items must include:

- Clear task owner (assigned individual or team)
- Specific deadline
- Verb-based description (e.g., "Gather task context from stakeholders" rather than "More task context")
- Success criteria for measuring completion

Asana's framework specifies that action items should start with a verb and include a deadline, making accountability trackable[1].

Source: https://www.wrike.com/scrum-guide/sprint-retrospective/ and https://asana.com/resources/sprint-retrospective

## Finding 3: ROI Measurement Through Iterative Tracking

**Scrum Master Junaid Shaikh** (March 2026) recommends creating "tiger teams" for specific issues, running small iterative experiments, and measuring whether trends improve in the next retrospective[4]. This approach treats action items as experiments with measurable outcomes rather than one-time fixes.

The methodology involves:
- Identifying specific metrics to track (e.g., sprint velocity, cycle time, defect rates)

- Re-evaluating success in the following retrospective

## Finding 4: Data-Driven Retrospective Analysis

Wrike recommends supporting retrospective discussions with **real data** rather than anecdotal feedback, including:

- Gantt charts to identify bottlenecks
- Task conversation logs to understand how roadblocks were navigated
- Sprint reports showing velocity, cycle time, and completion rates

This data-driven approach allows teams to measure whether action items actually resolved the underlying issues[2].

Source: https://www.wrike.com/scrum-guide/sprint-retrospective/

## Finding 5: Action Item Follow-Up and Closure Loop

Best practice requires reviewing action items from the previous retrospective at the start of the next one to evaluate whether issues were resolved or need revisiting[2]. This creates a continuous improvement cycle where ROI is measured by comparing metrics before and after action item implementation.

The 4Ls Retrospective framework emphasizes "following up regularly" to review action items in the next retrospective and "close the loop" to show progress[3].

Source: https://www.wrike.com/scrum-guide/sprint-retrospective/ and https://creately.com/guides/4ls-retrospective/
