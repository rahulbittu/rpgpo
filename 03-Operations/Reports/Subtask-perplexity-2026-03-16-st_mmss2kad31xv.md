## Finding 1: GutMIND Framework Identifies 9 Core Neuropsychiatric-Protective Microbiota via Gut-Brain Axis
- Published October 2026 in Gut Microbes journal; multi-cohort machine learning analysis across ASD, MDD, PD, SCZ cohorts revealed **9 core protective species** (e.g., health-abundant taxa linked to glutamate synthesis and acetate production).
- **Ruthenibacterium lactatiformans** enriched in patient cohorts for ASD, PD, SCZ; **108 metabolic pathways** altered (FDR-adjusted) across four disorders.

## Finding 2: Stanford Study Links Parabacteroides goldsteinii to Cognitive Decline via Vagus Nerve (March 2026)
- Mouse study (Stanford Medicine/Arc Institute, published March 2026) showed **Parabacteroides goldsteinii** increase in aging gut produces medium-chain fatty acids, triggering myeloid cell inflammation that inhibits vagus nerve, hippocampus activity, and memory formation.
- Vagus nerve activation in old mice restored cognitive performance to young levels; pathway: GI aging → microbial/metabolic shifts → myeloid inflammation → vagus impairment → memory decline.
- Human translation underway; vagus stimulation FDA-approved for depression/epilepsy/stroke.
- Source: https://med.stanford.edu/news/all-news/2026/03/gut-brain-cognitive-decline.html[2]

## Finding 3: Emory Study: High-Fat Diets Enable Gut Bacteria Translocation to Brain via Vagus Nerve (March 12, 2026)
- Mouse models (Emory University, PLOS Biology March 2026) on high-fat Paigen diet showed live gut bacteria enter brain via vagus nerve due to increased intestinal permeability; **47%** of vagotomized mice had brain bacteria vs. **75%** sham controls (3-4x lower levels).
- Reversible: normal diet reduced brain bacterial load; detected in Alzheimer's, Parkinson's, ASD models on standard diet.
- Implications: gut as therapy target for neurological conditions; co-PIs David Weiss (microbiologist) and Arash Grakoui.
- Source: https://news.emory.edu/stories/2026/03/hs_gut_bacteria_in_brain_connection_12-03-20261/story.html[3]; full paper https://journals.plos.org/plosbiology/article?id=10.1371%2Fjournal.pbio.3003652[4]

## Finding 4: Multi-Omics in 326 ASD Children Links Clostridioides difficile to Brain/Behavior (2026)
- Study (Fudan University/Xinhua Hospital, 326 ASD vs. 169 TD children aged 0-10) used neuroimaging, microbiome, metabolome; **Clostridioides difficile** abundance strongest predictor of ASD severity, brain structure variations.
- Age-dependent: differences diminish with age, converging to TD; mediation via microbes → metabolites → brain/behavior.
- Calls for early, age-stratified microbiome modulation therapies.
- Source: https://pubmed.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/41806837/[5]
