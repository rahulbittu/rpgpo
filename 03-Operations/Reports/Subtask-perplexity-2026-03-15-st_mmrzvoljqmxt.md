## Finding 1: The Score Takes Care of Itself: My Philosophy of Leadership
- **Author:** Bill Walsh  
- **Why Significant for Engineering Leaders:** Defines excellence standards for every role (e.g., receptionists to executives), preventing complacency after wins; emphasizes teaching over demanding to build team growth—key for engineering managers scaling teams amid AI risks and rapid tech changes in 2026. ## Finding 2: Leaders Eat Last: Together is Better (Inferred from Circle of Safety context)
- **Author:** Simon Sinek  
- **Why Significant for Engineering Leaders:** Introduces "Circle of Safety" to shield teams from internal threats like toxicity or layoffs, fostering trust for innovation; engineering leaders use this to prioritize employee well-being over metrics, reducing turnover in high-stress tech environments.[3]  
**Source:** https://ca.indeed.com/career-advice/career-development/books-on-management

## Finding 3: The 7 Habits of Highly Effective People: Powerful Lessons in Personal Change
- **Author:** Stephen R. Covey  
- **Why Significant for Engineering Leaders:** Builds inner self-mastery (e.g., proactive mindset, personal vision) before influencing teams; habits like "begin with the end in mind" align engineering roadmaps with long-term goals, improving delegation and cross-functional collaboration.[3][6]  
**Source:** https://ca.indeed.com/career-advice/career-development/books-on-management

## Finding 4: The One Minute Manager
- **Author:** Kenneth Blanchard Ph.D. and Spencer Johnson M.D.  
- **Why Significant for Engineering Leaders:** Teaches quick feedback techniques (one-minute goals, praises, redirects) for busy managers; engineering leaders apply this for daily standups and code reviews to boost performance without micromanaging.[3]  
**Source:** https://ca.indeed.com/career-advice/career-development/books-on-management

## Finding 5: Good to Great
- **Author:** Jim Collins  
- **Why Significant for Engineering Leaders:** Details Level 5 Leadership (humble yet driven) and Hedgehog Concept (focus on what you're best at); engineering teams use this for disciplined hiring, "right people on the bus," and sustained tech innovation over hype.[6]  
**Source:** https://www.ssbm.ch/top-10-must-read-books-for-business-management-students/
