## Detailed Analysis
- **Incentive Stock Options (ISOs)**: ISOs are attractive due to their tax advantages, as they do not incur regular income tax at any stage until the sale, provided holding requirements are met. However, the AMT can be a significant consideration if the spread between the exercise price and FMV is large at the time of exercise. The $100,000 limit on exercisable value per year necessitates careful planning to maximize benefits while avoiding AMT pitfalls.
  
- **Non-Qualified Stock Options (NSOs)**: NSOs are taxed at exercise, with the spread being treated as ordinary income. This can lead to substantial tax liabilities if the FMV is high at exercise. However, post-exercise appreciation is taxed as capital gains, which can be beneficial if the stock value increases significantly after exercise.

- **Section 83(b) Election**: This election is a strategic tool for NSO holders who exercise options early. By electing to pay taxes on the spread at the time of exercise when the FMV is low, employees can potentially reduce future tax liabilities on appreciation. The election must be filed within 30 days of exercise, making timely action critical.

- **Valuation and Timing**: The FMV at exercise or grant directly affects tax liabilities. Accurate valuations are crucial, especially for startups where valuations can fluctuate significantly. Employees should be aware of the timing of exercises and sales to optimize tax outcomes.
