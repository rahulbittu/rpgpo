## Finding 1: HSA-Eligible HDHP Requirements and Contribution Limits for 2026
HSA-eligible high-deductible health plans (HDHPs) require a minimum deductible of $1,700 for individuals or $3,400 for families in 2026. Maximum HSA contributions are $4,400 for self-only coverage and $8,750 for family coverage (plus $1,000 catch-up if age 55+).[2][4]
- All Bronze and catastrophic ACA Marketplace plans are HDHPs starting in 2026, making them HSA-eligible without additional major medical coverage.
- HSA contributions reduce modified adjusted gross income (MAGI) for ACA subsidies; example: $1,000 HSA contribution drops MAGI from $85,000 to $84,000, unlocking zero-premium Bronze plans (5 available at that income) and saving ~$34,000 in annual premiums for a couple.
Source: https://hsaforamerica.com/blog/health-insurance-for-gig-workers/[2]; https://www.healthinsurance.org/faqs/how-might-my-tax-deductions-affect-the-size-of-my-aca-premium-subsidy/[4]

## Finding 3: Group and Alternative HSA-Compatible Plans for Self-Employed/Small Businesses in Texas
HSA-compatible group plans feature lower premiums/higher deductibles; employers/employees fund HSAs pre-tax (2026 limits: >$4,400 individual, >$8,750 family).[1]
- Small businesses (<50 employees) not required to offer in Texas; alternatives like QSEHRA reimburse up to $537.50/month self-only or $1,091.66/month family.
- Traditional group costs: $9,325 single/$26,993 family annually (KFF data); alternatives cut costs 30-50%.
- Texas-specific: No mandate; self-employed/1099 contractors use private PPO alternatives if unsubsidized.
