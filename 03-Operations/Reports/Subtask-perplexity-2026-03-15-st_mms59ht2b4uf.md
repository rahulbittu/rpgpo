## Finding 1: Calisthenics Exercise Progressions via Harder Variations
PureGym outlines specific bodyweight progressions by advancing to more challenging versions without equipment:
- **Squats progression:** Bodyweight squats → split squats → pistol squats. Add challenge by slowing tempo, pausing, or pulsing.[2]
- **Push-ups progression:** Push-ups → diamond push-ups → single-arm push-ups.[2]
- **Pull-ups progression:** Pull-ups → eccentric pull-ups → weighted pull-ups (if adding load).[2]
- **Dips progression:** Tricep dips → chest dips → weighted dips.[2]
- **Push-ups to handstand progression:** Pike push-ups → handstand push-ups.[2]
**Source:** https://www.puregym.com/blog/calisthenics-workout-guide/

## Finding 2: Progressive Overload Principles for Bodyweight Training
Zing Coach applies progressive overload to bodyweight-inclusive routines by tracking and incrementally improving performance:
- Add 1 rep, slightly increase difficulty (e.g., slower tempo), or reduce rest time weekly.[1]
- Log sets, reps, and form in an app; aim for small weekly gains like 1 more rep on main movements.[1]
- Start with 3-day splits to build capacity before higher volume, prioritizing form via video feedback.[1]
**Source:** https://www.zing.coach/fitness-library/best-workout-split-for-beginners

## Finding 3: Hybrid Progression with Weights for Calisthenics Limits
PureGym recommends combining bodyweight progressions with weights to overcome plateaus:
- Use weights to build strength, making advanced calisthenics (e.g., pistol squats) easier.[2]
- Sample weekly plan: Monday (full body calisthenics), Wednesday (full body weights), Friday (alternating weights/calisthenics), with cardio/core/rest days.[2]
**Source:** https://www.puregym.com/blog/calisthenics-workout-guide/
