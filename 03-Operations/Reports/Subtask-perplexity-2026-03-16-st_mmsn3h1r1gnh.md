## Finding 1: ISAPP Consensus Definition of Gut Health (Published Feb 18, 2026)
Expert panel from International Scientific Association for Probiotics and Prebiotics (ISAPP) defines gut health as "a state of normal gastrointestinal function without active gastrointestinal disease and gut-related symptoms that affect quality of life." This covers functional (e.g., digestion, bowel habits), subjective (e.g., bloating, pain), and extrinsic (e.g., microbiome balance) domains; proposes clinical metrics like Bristol Stool Scale for bowel function, symptom questionnaires (e.g., GSRS), and quality-of-life scales (e.g., PAC-SYM). Limited reliable microbiome tests noted, emphasizing need for better tools in probiotic/diet studies.
- Source: https://pubmed.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/41709019/

## Finding 2: UC Davis Summary of ISAPP Consensus (Reported 2024, References 2026 Paper)
Panel led by Maria Marco (UC Davis) and Eamonn E. Quigley (Houston Methodist) in September 2024; stresses assessing gut health beyond labs/microbiome to include daily function and symptoms. Recommends framework for physicians/researchers evaluating probiotics, prebiotics, fermented foods; notes misalignment between symptoms/disease (e.g., remission in IBD/celiac still allows gut health).
- Source: https://www.ucdavis.edu/health/news/what-gut-health

## Finding 3: 2025-2030 Dietary Guidelines for Americans on Gut Microbiome
Guidelines explicitly recognize gut microbiome's role in health, promoting dietary patterns rich in fiber (28g/day women, 34g/day men), fermented foods (yogurt, kefir, kimchi), and diverse plants (30+ types/week) to support microbial diversity and SCFA production. Strengths: evidence from RCTs showing reduced inflammation; inconsistencies: lacks strain-specific probiotic dosing or tracking metrics.
- Source: https://www.gutmicrobiotaforhealth.com/the-ins-and-outs-of-the-new-dietary-guidelines-for-americans-in-light-of-the-gut-microbiome/

## Finding 4: Best/Worst Foods for Gut Health (2026 Practical Guide by RD)
**Best foods**: Oats (beta-glucan prebiotic, 5g/serving), garlic/onions (inulin, 2-5g/100g), kefir (10^9 CFU Lactobacillus/serving), berries (polyphenols, 200-500mg/100g), nuts (walnuts, 2-4g fiber/oz). **Worst foods**: Ultra-processed (chips, sodas; <10% fiber), artificial sweeteners (aspartame disrupts Bifidobacteria per 2025 meta-analysis), excessive red meat (>500g/week linked to TMAO rise). Real-life tip: Swap 1 processed snack/day for kefir + berries; track via app for 4 weeks.
- Source: https://dev.catalog.calpia.ca.gov/custom/assets/today/news.html?p=best-and-worst-foods-for-gut-health-a-2026-real-life-guide-from-a-working-rd-69afe3cd05db4

## Finding 5: Evidence on Probiotics and Microbial Therapeutics (Frontiers Review, Accepted Mar 11, 2026)
Diet-based modulation: High-fiber (30-50g/day) + fermented foods increase SCFAs (acetate, propionate, butyrate) by 20-50% in 4-12 weeks (RCT data). Probiotics: Strain-specific like Bifidobacterium longum BB536 (10^9 CFU/day) improves IBS symptoms in 70% of users (2025 meta-analysis); prebiotics (inulin-FOS 5-10g/day) boost Bifidobacteria 1-log. Postbiotics (heat-killed Lactobacillus, 10^10 cells/day) reduce inflammation markers (CRP -15%). Multi-omics tracking recommended for progress.
- Source: https://www.frontiersin.org/journals/microbiology/articles/10.3389/fmicb.2026.1776983/full
