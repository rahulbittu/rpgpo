## Claiming Strategies
Claiming Social Security at age 62 reduces benefits by up to 30% if full retirement age (FRA) is 67, while delaying past FRA until age 70 increases benefits by 8% annually.[4][6] The maximum benefit in 2026 is $5,181 monthly ($62,172 yearly) at age 70.[4] For high-net-worth individuals, claiming at 62 or FRA and investing proceeds conservatively (4-5% pre-tax return) can accumulate ~$220,000 liquid capital by age 70, often outperforming delay strategies until age 88-90.[2]

## Spousal Benefits
Spouses can claim up to 50% of the higher earner's FRA benefit, even with limited work history; e.g., lower-earning spouse gets $874/month on own record at FRA, switching to 50% of higher earner's after they claim at 70.[5] Early claiming by primary earner reduces spousal benefit by 35% (e.g., $500 FRA drops to $325 at 62).[6] Coordinate: higher earner delays to 70, lower claims own at FRA then switches.[3][5]

**Next steps:** Use SSA spousal calculator at ssa.gov/benefits/retirement/planner/claiming.html; discuss with spouse using example like Harry/Helen scenario ($874 base).[5]

## Working While Receiving Benefits
Earnings above $65,160 pre-FRA birthday month in FRA year trigger $1 deduction per $3 over limit; no penalty after FRA month.[4] Post-FRA, work freely while delaying claim to earn 8% annual credits until 70.[1][6] Replace low-earning years (e.g., college jobs) with higher pay via promotion, side hustle, or new job to boost record.[4]

**Next steps:** Track 2026 earnings limit updates at ssa.gov/benefits/retirement/planner/whileworking.html; aim for max taxable earnings ($176,100 estimated 2026 cap, pending announcement).
