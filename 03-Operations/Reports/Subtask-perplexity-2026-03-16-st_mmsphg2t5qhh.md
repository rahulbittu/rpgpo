## Light Exposure Timing
- **Morning bright light exposure** aligns circadian rhythms by suppressing melatonin and boosting alertness; circadian rhythm responds directly to light as the primary zeitgeber (time-giver).[6]
- Women experience melatonin rise around **9 PM**, peaking at **3 AM**; men ~1 hour later—avoid late evening light to preserve this peak.[4]
- High-intensity exercise in **late afternoon** raises core temperature, which drops to signal sleep preparation, indirectly supporting light-driven rhythms.[4]

## Meal Timing
- Avoid dinner at **7-8 PM** for women to prevent digestion interfering with **9 PM** melatonin spike; focus on afternoon prep for parasympathetic activation and natural melatonin release.[4]
- Afternoon-initiated **24-hour fasts** (e.g., starting post-lunch) improve glycemic control by aligning with circadian rhythms, reducing metabolic stress vs. evening starts.[5]

## Exercise Timing
- **Late afternoon high-intensity exercise** elevates core temperature, aiding the evening drop that signals sleep onset and strengthens circadian cues.[4]
- Consistent daily routines amplify exercise benefits for rhythm stability, though specific timings tie to individual melatonin profiles (women earlier than men).[4]

## Sleep Architecture Improvement
- **CRS digital therapeutic app** (Circadian Rhythm for Sleep) uses wearable data for real-time circadian realignment: analyzes sleep-wake patterns via ML algorithm, delivers personalized z-score feedback and daily recommendations; pilot showed improved subjective/objective sleep parameters with high adherence via passive tracking.[1]
- **CORTI sweat sensor** by EnLiSense (UT Austin study, Oct 2025 issue of Biosensors and Bioelectronics: X) monitors cortisol (wake-promoting) and melatonin in sweat over 48 hours; matched saliva samples in 43 participants, enabling circadian data for sleep optimization beyond motion/HR wearables.[2]
- Fixed bed/wake times within **±30 minutes** (e.g., 9:30-10:30 PM bedtime) build strong rhythms; anchor wake time tighter for better immune/metabolic attunement—shift workers risk desynchrony leading to infections, diabetes.[3][4]
- Poor sleep triggers next-day overeating of energy-dense foods; brain "cleans" in microdoses daytime if overnight fails, causing energy/blood sugar fluctuations.[4]
