## Claiming Age Strategies for Couples
Higher-earning spouses should delay claiming until age 70 to maximize their own benefit (up to 77% increase from age 62, or 24-32% from full retirement age) and survivor benefits for the surviving spouse, who receives the larger of the two benefits.[1][4][6] Lower-earning spouses may claim earlier (e.g., age 62) for spousal benefits at 50% of the higher earner's full retirement age (FRA) benefit, but early claiming permanently reduces this amount—e.g., from $2,071/month at FRA to $1,400/month at 62, costing couples $800-$1,200/month over 20 years.[2]

**Next Steps:** Use SSA's online calculator at ssa.gov/benefits/calculators to model your Primary Insurance Amounts (PIAs); input both spouses' earnings histories and test scenarios like higher earner at 70, lower at 62.[2]

## Spousal Benefit Coordination
Coordinate by having the lower earner claim spousal benefits early while the higher earner delays, treating the higher benefit as the "backbone" for lifetime and survivor protection; this avoids permanent reductions in spousal benefits if both claim early.[1][2] Survivor benefits prioritize the higher earner's delayed amount—e.g., husband at 70 gets ~$4,000/month vs. $2,250 at 62, benefiting the surviving wife longest.[3][4]

**Next Steps:** Check eligibility for ex-spousal benefits if married 10+ years via SSA account; run joint scenarios before filing to map interactions.[2]

## Break-Even Analysis
Break-even focuses on survivor income, not joint retirement: delaying higher earner's benefit offsets post-death expense drops and portfolio conservatism, as survivors hold more cash/reduce stocks, slowing growth.[3] Example: Claiming at 62 vs. 70 trades early payments for higher lifelong guaranteed income; breakeven ~age 80-82 assuming average longevity, but survivor focus shifts priority to delay.[1][3]

**Next Steps:** Build scenarios listing expenses (housing, healthcare) vs. guaranteed income at ages 62/67/70; apply cash flow, tax, and survivor filters using Excel or SSA tools.[1]

## Impact of Continued Work
In 2026, earnings over $24,480 before FRA reduce benefits $1 per $2 earned, potentially wiping out early claims—e.g., a working spouse earning above limit loses thousands yearly.[2] Avoid early claiming if working; delay preserves full benefits and avoids penalties.[2]

**Next Steps:** Calculate 2026 earnings test via SSA worksheet; if over limit, postpone claiming until FRA (67 for most).[2]
