## Types of REITs
REITs divide into **equity REITs**, which own and manage properties like landlords (e.g., collecting rent on offices, apartments, warehouses, retail), and **mortgage REITs**, which invest in mortgage-backed securities.[3][2]  
Subcategories include **publicly traded REITs** (exchange-listed, liquid), **public nontraded REITs** (SEC-registered but illiquid, often 8+ years lockup, available via brokers like Fundrise), and others like specialty REITs (e.g., Innovative Industrial Properties Inc. (IIPR) for cannabis facilities, owning 112 properties in 19 states as of June).[1][2]  
Examples: Healthcare (CareTrust REIT (CTRE), senior housing), retail (Four Corners Property Trust (FCPT)), multifamily (NexPoint Residential Trust (NXRT), 36 properties in Phoenix/South Florida/Dallas), casinos (VICI Properties, Gaming & Leisure Properties), data centers/residential/self-storage (strong tailwinds).[1][3][4]

## Tax Treatment
REITs must distribute at least 90% of taxable income as dividends to avoid corporate taxes, passing income to shareholders who pay taxes at ordinary rates (not qualified dividends).[2]  
Depreciation impacts: Some REITs like Lamar Advertising (51% of 2025 dividends from depreciation) face scrutiny, but screened REITs limit this to under 50%.[3]
