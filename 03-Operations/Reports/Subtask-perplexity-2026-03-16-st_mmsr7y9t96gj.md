## Finding 1: Build Relationships with Key Board Recruiters for Personal Access
Board recruitment firms in the UK maintain extensive networks to source directors; effective strategy involves building ongoing relationships with recruiters by maintaining contact lists, following them on LinkedIn, and updating them on your progress to stay top-of-mind for assignments. ## Finding 2: Conduct Board Skills Analysis Before Recruiting Advisors
Proactively analyze your personal board's skills matrix against future needs (e.g., tech disruption, risks) to identify gaps, rather than relying on networks alone; this may lead to targeted recruitment, director education, or external expertise instead of new additions. Competition for experienced advisors is intense, so align criteria with strategy and ensure cultural fit through disciplined processes.[3][6]

## Finding 3: Use Retained Search and Pipeline Refresh for High-Quality Matches
Boards achieve better outcomes with retained search firms offering confidential mapping, short-list management, and cultural insight; maintain a refreshed talent pipeline of pre-evaluated external candidates (e.g., cross-sector leaders) to reduce reactive hiring—McKinsey notes 20-30% of critical roles lack optimal fits due to familiarity bias. Implement behavioral assessments, multi-perspective interviews, and scenario simulations to reveal decision-making under pressure.[2]  
**Source:** https://novoexec.com/news/rethinking-executive-appointments-why-boards-must-adapt-their-approach/

## Finding 4: Test for Judgment and AI/Tech Expertise in 2026 Context
Prioritize advisors with proven judgment in uncertainty (e.g., P&L experience, cyber/AI knowledge from operator roles, not just specialists); probe decision narratives on trade-offs and adaptations. Boards expect periodic refreshment to align with evolving needs like AI oversight, including education or recruiting experts—avoid over-reliance on track records.[4][5][7]
