# Municipal Bonds for High-Income Earners: Tax-Equivalent Yields and Market Opportunities

**Municipal bonds currently offer compelling after-tax returns for high-income earners, with taxable-equivalent yields around 5% or higher compared to 2.5-3% coupon yields, making them particularly attractive relative to Treasuries and corporate bonds.**[1][4]

## Tax-Equivalent Yield Analysis

The tax advantage for high-income earners is substantial. For investors in the highest marginal tax brackets, a municipal bond yielding 2.5-3% on a coupon basis translates to a **taxable-equivalent yield of approximately 5%**—the return you'd need to earn in taxable fixed income to match the after-tax benefit.[4] This comparison is critical because it shows the true economic value of tax-exempt income.

As of February 12, 2026, the **Bloomberg Municipal Bond Index was up 1.5% year-to-date**, representing one of the strongest starts to a year since 2020.[6] However, the relative value varies significantly by maturity. For bonds with maturities under 10 years, the **muni-to-Treasury ratio is approximately 64%**, meaning municipal yields are relatively tight compared to Treasuries.[5] In contrast, the **30-year muni-to-Treasury ratio stands at roughly 87%**, offering a **yield advantage of over 100 basis points (1.0%) after taxes** compared to shorter-maturity bonds.[6]

## Steep Yield Curve Advantage

The municipal bond yield curve remains **unusually steep**, with the spread between short-term and long-term municipals widening significantly. Two years ago, this spread was approximately 80 basis points; **today it stands at about 130 basis points**.[4] This steepness creates a compelling opportunity: investors can lock in returns on long-term municipal bonds that are approximately equal to Treasury yields while maintaining federal tax exemption—and potentially state tax exemption depending on residency.[2]

**Longer-maturity municipal bonds in the 15-20 year segment delivered the highest returns** in recent months, outperforming intermediate and shorter-maturity bonds.[3]

## Credit Quality and Performance

The municipal bond market entered 2026 with strong momentum supported by steady fund flows and stable credit fundamentals.[3] Performance data shows that **BBB and A-rated credits outpaced higher-quality AAA bonds**, while **high-yield municipal bonds outperformed investment-grade bonds for the second consecutive month** to start 2026.[3] This suggests opportunities exist beyond the highest-rated securities, though with corresponding credit risk.

Municipal bonds have **extremely low default rates**, making them a high-quality diversification tool for equity-heavy portfolios.[4]

## Income Threshold Considerations

While higher-income earners benefit most from tax-exempt bonds, the current yield environment makes municipals attractive even for taxpayers in **marginal tax brackets around 17%**—lower than the highest brackets.[1] However, investors should be aware of potential trade-offs: municipal bond income is added back into calculations for Medicare IRMAA surcharges, may increase taxable Social Security income, and could trigger Alternative Minimum Tax (AMT) exposure for some investors.[1]

## Active vs. Passive Management

A key distinction in the municipal bond space is the difference between active and passive management. **Longer-dated municipals continue to offer attractive relative value versus Treasuries, highlighting the benefits of active management for income-focused portfolios.**[3] This suggests that security selection and tactical positioning matter significantly in capturing the full opportunity set.

## Market Context: California Demand

California municipal bonds are experiencing exceptional demand as investors seek tax-free income to offset high state income taxes. California's recent bond offering included **$1.4 billion in general obligation bonds and $1.1 billion in refunding bonds**, led by JPMorgan Chase and RBC Capital Markets, with proceeds directed toward behavioral health infrastructure, K-12 education, disaster preparedness, and high-speed rail.[5]
