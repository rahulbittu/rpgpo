## Finding 1: Wearable-Driven Digital Therapeutics for Insomnia

**Circadian Rhythm for Sleep (CRS)** is a novel mobile app-based digital therapeutic that uses real-time wearable data to deliver personalized circadian interventions for insomnia[1]. Unlike traditional sleep interventions focused on cognitive behavioral therapy or mindfulness, CRS uniquely incorporates **continuous, individualized circadian feedback** derived from wearable devices, preventing subjective bias[1].

The app uses a proprietary machine learning algorithm that analyzes individual sleep-wake and circadian patterns to produce daily sleep health forecasts and personalized behavioral recommendations[1]. Features include a "circadian coaching" page offering tailored guidance based on adherence to behavioral targets across sleep quality, light exposure, activity regularity, and circadian phase stability[1].

**Source:** https://pmc.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/articles/PMC12961147/

## Finding 2: Daylight Saving Time Disrupts Circadian Alignment and Increases Migraines

Recent research from **UC Davis Health** (March 2026) demonstrates that even a **one-hour clock change** significantly disrupts circadian alignment with measurable health consequences[2]:

- **Migraine frequency nearly doubled** after the spring time change: migraine days increased from **7.76 to 13.35 per 100 person-days**[2]
- **Deep sleep decreased** from approximately **94 minutes per night to 84 minutes** after the time change[2]
- Total sleep time remained unchanged, indicating selective disruption of deep sleep architecture[2]

Lead researcher **Dr. Sasikanth Gorantla**, a UC Davis neurologist and sleep expert, emphasized that "circadian stability is essential for migraine management"[2]. The American Academy of Sleep Medicine recommends **year-round standard time** because it better supports circadian alignment and overall health[2].

**Source:** https://health.ucdavis.edu/welcome/news/headlines/daylight-saving-time-triggers-more-migraines-cuts-deep-sleep/2026/03

## Finding 3: Practical Strategies for Circadian Realignment During Time Changes

**Dr. Roopa Shah**, an SSM Health Family Medicine Physician and wellness coach, recommends a gradual adjustment approach[3]:

- **Pre-change preparation**: Shift your schedule by going to bed **15-20 minutes earlier each day** in the week before the time change, adjusting meals and exercise accordingly[3]
- **Light exposure**: Wake up **15 minutes earlier** each day and get morning sunlight exposure, as sunlight is the most effective regulator of circadian rhythms[6]
- **Strategic napping**: If needed, take naps lasting **less than 30 minutes in early afternoon** to avoid disrupting nighttime sleep[3]

## Finding 4: Blue Light and Doomscrolling Disrupt Sleep Quality

A 2026 poll from the American Academy of Sleep Medicine (AASM) found that **doomscrolling significantly affects sleep**, particularly in younger demographics[4]:

- **43% of adults aged 25-34** report doomscrolling affects their sleep
- **39% of adults aged 35-44** report negative sleep effects
- **38% of middle-aged adults** report sleep disruption from doomscrolling[4]

Blue light combined with emotionally charged content "tricks our body clocks into a state of daytime-level alertness, disrupting the circadian rhythm"[4]. The AASM recommends[4]:

- Avoid blue light from handheld electronics **30-60 minutes before bedtime**
- Leave phones in another room when sleeping
- Turn off phone notifications to prevent sleep disturbances

**Source:** https://www.powershealth.org/about-us/newsroom/health-library/2026/02/25/doomscrolling-affecting-many-americans-sleep-poll-finds

## Finding 5: Sleep Deprivation and Age-Related Circadian Changes

Research on older adults reveals that circadian disruption has serious health consequences[7]:

- Sleep disturbances are not a normal part of aging, but age-related changes to circadian rhythms make it harder to fall and stay asleep[7]
- **Shortened sleep (fewer than 5 hours)** in women age 70+ increases risk for falls and fractures[7]
- Men age 65+ with interrupted or shortened sleep demonstrate poorer physical functioning measured by grip strength and gait speed[7]
- **20-60% of older adults** report taking daytime naps to compensate for poor overnight sleep[7]

The AASM recommends maintaining a **consistent sleep schedule**, designating the bedroom for sleep only, and avoiding caffeine and alcohol close to bedtime[7].

**Source:** https://www.ncoa.org/article/sleep-statistics-older-adults/
