## Bedroom Setup
- Keep the bedroom **dark, quiet, and cool** to support sleep onset and quality; invest in a good mattress and pillows for proper posture.[4]
- Limit screen exposure at least 1 hour before bed to avoid blue light interference with melatonin production.[4]

## Pre-Sleep Routines
- Establish a consistent bedtime ritual including reading, meditating, gentle stretching, or deep breathing to calm the mind and regulate the body clock.[4]
- Avoid heavy meals, caffeine, alcohol, and sugar close to bedtime; opt for light snacks if needed.[4]
- Practice relaxation techniques like journaling or mindfulness to manage stress, a major sleep disruptor.[4]
- Physical exercise programs (e.g., 12-week moderate-intensity aerobic + resistance training, 3x50min weekly sessions) improve sleep latency, efficiency, and quality per RCT with 40 participants (Tseng et al., 2020).[1]

## Wake Optimization
- Maintain a regular sleep-wake schedule daily to align circadian rhythm and enhance restorative sleep.[4]
- Stay active during the day with regular physical activity (e.g., aerobic, tai chi, resistance, aquatic) to fall asleep faster and deepen sleep; 7 studies (984 participants) showed gains in total sleep time and reduced wake after onset.[1]
- Optimal weekday sleep: **7 hours 18 minutes** linked to peak insulin sensitivity (eGDR 8.23 average); avoid >2 hours weekend catch-up if exceeding this, as it lowers eGDR.[3]

## Tracking Metrics
- Monitor **sleep latency** (time to fall asleep), **sleep efficiency** (time asleep vs. in bed), **total sleep time**, and **wake after sleep onset** using actigraphy or polysomnography; meta-analyses confirm exercise benefits here.[1]
- Track PSQI (Pittsburgh Sleep Quality Index) scores and subjective quality; significant improvements in intervention groups vs. controls.[1]
- Log progress on sleep goals, caffeine intake, and mood; use apps or journals for consistency.[4]
- For adolescents, NSF's March 3, 2026 recommendations (via Sleep Health journal) emphasize evidence-based tracking across healthcare/education; full text at sleephealthjournal.org.[2]
