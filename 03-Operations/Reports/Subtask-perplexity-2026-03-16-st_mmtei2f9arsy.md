## Finding 1: Progressive Loading Protocol for Ankle Rehabilitation

**Specific Protocol:**
Physical therapists recommend a structured progression starting with **gentle range of motion exercises** (ankle circles, dorsiflexion, plantarflexion, alphabet tracing with your foot) in the first 1-2 days post-injury[3]. Once you can walk without pain—typically within the first week for Grade I sprains—progress to **strengthening exercises including calf raises, resistance band exercises for peroneal muscles, and single-leg balance work**[3].

**Exercise Details:**

- **Banded Fibularis Longus/Brevis Strengthening (Eversion):** Sit with legs extended, loop a light resistance band around the ball of your foot, slowly turn the sole outward against resistance, perform **15–20 slow, controlled reps per side**. Increase band resistance as strength builds[1].

- **Banded Posterior Tibialis Strengthening (Inversion):** Sit in figure four position, loop band around the ball of the top foot, slowly turn sole upward against resistance, perform **15–20 slow, controlled reps per side**[1].

- **Single-Leg Heel Raise with Pause:** Stand on one foot, rise onto the ball of your foot as high as possible, pause for **2 seconds** at the top, lower with full control. Perform **3 sets of 12–15 reps per side**[1].

Source: https://www.therapeuticassociates.com/bulletproof-ankles-how-do-i-prevent-repeating-an-ankle-sprain/

## Finding 2: Proprioception Training (Balance and Stability)

**Critical Final Phase:**
Balance and proprioception training is described as **"non-negotiable"** for preventing re-injury[3]. The progression includes:

- Single-leg standing
- Wobble board work
- Single-leg hops (multi-directional, once pain-free)

These exercises retrain the ankle's ability to react quickly to unstable surfaces[3]. Runners should be able to complete **5 sets of 20 seconds in each direction without pain, wobbling, or loss of control** before progressing to running[3].

## Finding 3: Return-to-Running Criteria

**Readiness Assessment:**
Before returning to running, runners must demonstrate:

- Ability to walk without pain (typically within the first week for Grade I sprains)
- Completion of strengthening phase without symptoms
- **5 sets of 20 seconds of single-leg balance work in each direction without pain, wobbling, or loss of control**[3]

The search results emphasize that "a few extra days of patience now can save you weeks of setbacks later," indicating that rushing return-to-running significantly increases re-injury risk[3].

Source: https://kinetic-revolution.com/running-sprained-ankle

## Finding 4: Recovery Timeline for Different Injury Types

**General Recovery Expectations:**
- **Mild overuse injuries:** May improve within a few weeks
- **Tendon injuries or stress reactions:** Can require several months of progressive loading and structured rehabilitation[4]

For Achilles tendinopathy specifically, research protocols involve **12-week progressive loading interventions**, with high-load groups starting at 55% of 1 repetition maximum (1RM) and progressively increasing to 90% 1RM over 12 weeks[2].
