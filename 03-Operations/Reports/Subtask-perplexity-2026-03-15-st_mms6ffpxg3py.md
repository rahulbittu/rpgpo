## Therac-25 Radiation Therapy Machine Bugs
Software bugs in the Therac-25 machine caused massive radiation overdoses, killing at least three patients and severely injuring others between 1985 and 1987. The bugs involved race conditions where high-dose electron beams were delivered without proper safety interlocks due to faulty error handling and operator interface issues; one incident on December 26, 1985, at the Medical College of Georgia delivered 16,500-25,000 rads (intended: 200 rads), causing patient agony and death weeks later.[1]

## Ariane 5 Rocket Explosion
On June 4, 1996, the European Space Agency's Ariane 5 rocket, valued at US$370 million (prototype cost ~US$1 billion program), self-destructed 37 seconds after launch due to a software bug in the inertial reference system. A 64-bit floating-point number (horizontal bias velocity) exceeded 32-bit integer limits during reuse of Ariane 4 code, triggering an operand error and shutdown of the backup system, causing loss of attitude control.[1]

## RAF Chinook Helicopter Crash
On June 2, 1994, a Royal Air Force Chinook helicopter (ZD576) crashed into the Mull of Kintyre, Scotland, killing all 29 on board (25 intelligence officers, 4 crew). Initially ruled pilot error, a 2000 inquiry and 2011 review attributed it to a software bug in the engine control FADEC system, likely causing sudden dual-engine shutdown above 15,000 feet due to unhandled fault logic.[1]

## British Post Office Horizon Scandal
Between 1999 and 2015, faulty Fujitsu Horizon accounting software caused the Post Office to prosecute ~900 subpostmasters for theft/fraud, leading to 4 suicides, 236 imprisonments, and widespread bankruptcies/ruined lives. Bugs included duplicate transactions, unsent messages logged as cash shortfalls (e.g., £1,000 errors), and remote tampering capabilities; a 2024 inquiry confirmed software defects as primary cause.[1]
