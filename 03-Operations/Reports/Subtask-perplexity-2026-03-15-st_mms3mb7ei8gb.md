## Finding 1: 2026 401(k) Contribution Limits and Catch-Up Rules

**Specific limits for 2026:**

- Employer match: typically 3-6% of salary (varies by company)

## Finding 2: Roth IRA 2026 Contribution Limits and Income Phase-Out Thresholds

**2026 Roth IRA limits:**
- Annual contribution: **$7,000** (unchanged from 2025)
- Catch-up (age 50+): additional $1,000
- **Income phase-out ranges (single filers):**
  - Begins: $146,000
  - Completely phases out: $161,000
  - Married filing jointly: $230,000-$240,000

**Critical for tech professionals:** If your salary exceeds $146k (common for senior engineers, staff engineers, or managers), you cannot directly contribute to a Roth IRA. **Backdoor Roth strategy** becomes essential.

**Backdoor Roth process:**
1. Contribute $7,000 to traditional IRA (non-deductible)
2. Immediately convert to Roth IRA
3. Pay taxes only on gains between contribution and conversion (typically minimal)
4. No income limits on conversions

**Pro Mega Backdoor Roth:** If your 401(k) plan allows in-service conversions, you can contribute up to $69,000 total to traditional 401(k), then convert excess to Roth. Check with your HR/benefits team.

Source: https://www.irs.gov/retirement-plans/roth-iras

## Finding 3: Tax Implications and Effective Tax Planning for Tech Professionals

**Traditional 401(k) tax benefits:**
- Contributions reduce taxable income dollar-for-dollar
- At $200k salary in California (13.3% state tax), federal 24% bracket: **$37 saved per $100 contributed**
- Distributions in retirement taxed as ordinary income

**Roth IRA/Roth 401(k) tax benefits:**
- Contributions made with after-tax dollars
- **All growth and withdrawals are tax-free** (after age 59½, 5-year rule)
- No required minimum distributions (RMDs) during your lifetime
- Ideal for high earners expecting higher tax brackets in retirement

**2026 tax bracket context (single filer):**
- 24% bracket: $191,950-$243,725
- 32% bracket: $243,725-$609,350
- Most senior tech engineers fall in 24-32% range

**Strategic recommendation for tech professionals:**
- Max out 401(k) traditional contributions first (immediate tax deduction)
- Use backdoor Roth for additional $7,000 (tax-free growth)
- If eligible for Mega Backdoor Roth, prioritize Roth conversions (you're likely in high tax bracket now, but may be lower in retirement if you exit tech)

Source: https://www.irs.gov/newsroom/irs-provides-tax-inflation-adjustments-for-tax-year-2026

## Finding 5: Tech-Specific Considerations (Stock Options, RSUs, ESPP)

**Critical issue for tech professionals:** Concentrated equity risk

**Typical tech comp structure:**
- Base salary: $150-200k
- Stock options/RSUs: 50-200% of base (vests over 4 years)
- Bonus: 10-20%

**Tax implications:**
- **RSUs:** Taxed as ordinary income at vesting (not capital gains)
- **Stock options (ISOs):** Potentially qualified for long-term capital gains if held 2+ years post-exercise
- **ESPP:** Often 10-15% discount; gains taxed as ordinary income

**Action items:**
1. **Diversify immediately:** Sell RSUs upon vesting; don't hold concentrated positions
2. **Max out 401(k) first:** Reduces taxable income from RSU vesting
3. **Use Roth for diversified investments:** Keep company stock in taxable account for better tax-loss harvesting
4. **Consider mega backdoor Roth:** If you have significant RSU income, this is your best tax shelter

Source: https://www.investopedia.com/articles/personal-finance/081216/how-are-rsu-taxes-handled.asp
