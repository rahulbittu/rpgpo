# Board Games for Systems Thinking and Climate Education

Board games have emerged as powerful pedagogical tools for teaching systems thinking and climate change, with several specific games and educational programs demonstrating measurable learning outcomes.

## Key Games and Programs

**Daybreak** is a fully **cooperative climate-action board game** designed by Matteo Menapace and Matt Leacock that won Best Board or Tabletop Game for Impact at the 2024 Games for Change Awards[1]. The game mechanics require players to represent different world regions (Europe, the United States, China, and the Majority World) working toward "Drawdown"—the point where more carbon is removed from the atmosphere than emitted[1]. Players must cut emissions, invest in infrastructure and ecosystems, and prevent regional collapse, with the entire team losing if global temperatures exceed 2.0°C or any region collapses[1]. A companion game called **Dawn** is in development as a shorter, more accessible version focused on zero emissions[1].

**Solutions** is a collaborative board game based on climate science data from Project Drawdown that emphasizes data-driven solutions[2]. Players work together to assess technological and social innovations to reduce planetary temperature, with a win/lose dynamic that mirrors the need for global cooperation[2]. The game incorporates playful challenge cards—for example, one card requires the next round to be played in silence using only gestures[2].

**Climate Fresk** uses a 5×7 card system where players arrange cards into a flowchart, constructing a web of relations while discussing factors and outcomes[2]. Participants trace lines of connection to map relationships, emphasizing the systemic nature of climate crisis rather than individual blame[2].

## Educational Outcomes and Pedagogical Benefits

Research demonstrates that board game design for learning develops critical skills for climate leadership[2]. Students practicing game design develop **creative thinking, problem-solving, empathy, adaptability, and strategic foresight**—skills necessary for anticipating consequences, grappling with uncertainty, balancing competing interests, and communicating solutions[2].

At the London School of Economics, postgraduate students used board games to model climate-health policy trade-offs, with one student-designed game revealing that "survival depends on crossing health thresholds–reducing disease burden and maintaining healthcare capacity–while navigating cooperation dilemmas," concluding that "equity isn't a moral luxury, but a strategic condition for resilience"[1].

The Winchester School of Art's "Make Your Own Climate Board Game" workshop brought together climate activists, artists, designers, policymakers, researchers, students, and the public to prototype climate-themed games[3]. Participants reported that the game-design process fostered "deeper, more systemic understanding of the climate crisis"[3].

## Systemic Advantages

Board games offer distinct pedagogical advantages: their rules are visible and collectively negotiated by players; materials are readily available and accessible; and handling physical objects facilitates collaboration[3]. The iterative design process requires participants to empathize with players, define core experiences, prototype rapidly, and test assumptions—grounding learning in real-world system dynamics[3].

Menapace emphasizes that this approach is particularly valuable in low- and middle-income countries where climate impacts are acute and communities are often excluded from policymaking, as simplified, low-cost games can help people understand complex issues and adapt them to reflect lived realities[1].
