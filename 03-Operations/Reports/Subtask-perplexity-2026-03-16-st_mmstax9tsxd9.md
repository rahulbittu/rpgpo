## Backdoor Roth IRA Contributions (2026 Limits and Legality)
Backdoor Roth IRAs remain legal in 2026, allowing high earners to contribute up to **$7,500** ($8,600 if age 50+) to a nondeductible Traditional IRA then convert to Roth IRA, bypassing direct Roth income limits of $153,000–$168,000 (single) or $242,000–$252,000 (MFJ).[2][3]

- Process: Contribute after-tax dollars to Traditional IRA (up to earned income, max $7,500/$8,600), then convert to Roth; minimal tax if no pre-tax IRA balances exist.[1][5]
- Income eligibility: Full direct Roth allowed under $153,000 (single)/$242,000 (MFJ); phase-out $153,000–$168,000 (single)/$242,000–$252,000 (MFJ).[2]

## Pro-Rata Rule and Aggregation Rules
The **pro-rata rule** taxes conversions based on the ratio of after-tax IRA value to total IRA assets (all Traditional, SEP, SIMPLE IRAs aggregated at year-end); e.g., if after-tax is 20% of total, 20% of conversion is tax-free.[1]

- Aggregation applies across all your IRAs (not 401(k)s); roll pre-tax IRAs to employer plan first to isolate after-tax contributions and avoid pro-rata tax.[1]
- Married filers: Spouse's IRAs not aggregated with yours.[1]
- Rule of thumb: Taxable portion = (pre-tax IRA total / all IRA total) x conversion amount.[1]

## Form 8606 Filing
File **IRS Form 8606** to report nondeductible Traditional IRA contributions (Part I) and Roth conversions (Part II), calculating pro-rata taxable amount; required even if conversion is nontaxable.[3]

- Due with annual tax return; tracks basis to prevent double taxation.[3]
- Example: Report $7,500 nondeductible contribution, then conversion; pro-rata determines taxable gain if other pre-tax IRAs exist.[1][3]

## Mega Backdoor Roth through Employer Plans
**Mega Backdoor Roth** uses 401(k)s for after-tax contributions up to total plan limit of **$72,000** ($80,000 age 50+; super catch-up $11,250 for ages 60-63), after maxing elective deferrals (**$24,500** under 50; +$8,000 age 50+ or +$11,250 ages 60-63) and employer contributions.[2][3][4]

- Requirements: Plan must allow after-tax contributions and in-plan Roth conversions (or in-service distributions to Roth IRA).[4]
- 2026 compliance: Plans without Roth options lose catch-up contributions; amendments due by Dec 31, 2026, but operational now per 2025 regs.[4]
- Example: Under 50 worker defers $24,500 + $15,000 employer match leaves ~$32,500 after-tax room to convert to Roth.[4]
