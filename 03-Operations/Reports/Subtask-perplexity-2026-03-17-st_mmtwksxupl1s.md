## Backdoor Roth IRA Strategy Overview
The **backdoor Roth IRA** enables high-income earners to bypass 2026 Roth IRA direct contribution income limits (not specified in results but contributions capped at **$7,500** or **$8,600** if 50+) by making nondeductible contributions to a traditional IRA and immediately converting to a Roth IRA, as conversions have no income limits.[3][1][4]

## Step-by-Step Execution for High Earners
- **Step 1**: Contribute after-tax (nondeductible) dollars to a traditional IRA up to the annual limit ($7,500/$8,600 in 2026).[3]
- **Step 2**: Convert the contribution to a Roth IRA shortly after (same day or pay period ideal) to minimize taxable earnings accrual.[2][4]
- **Step 3**: File **IRS Form 8606** with your tax return to report the nondeductible basis and conversion; prior-year basis from 2024 Form 8606 line 14 must be tracked if applicable, with a **$50 penalty** for non-filing (waivable for reasonable cause). Mail standalone 2024 Form 8606 if needed without amending return.[1][3]
- **TurboTax Entry**: On "Review your 1099-R info" screen (search 1099-R), answer **Yes** to prior nondeductible contributions, input basis and IRA values.[1]

## Pro-Rata Rule Impact and Mitigation
The **pro-rata rule** taxes conversions proportionally across all traditional, SEP, and SIMPLE IRAs based on pre-tax vs. after-tax balances; existing pre-tax IRAs make backdoor conversions partially taxable, derailing the strategy for many high earners.[4][5][3]
- Roll pre-tax IRAs into an employer 401(k) before converting to isolate the nondeductible contribution.[4][5]
- Calculate total IRA basis beforehand; applies to 401(k) conversions too.[5]

**Source**: https://navalign.com/backdoor-roth-ira-strategy/[4]; https://www.clrvw.com/roth-conversion-strategies-when-and-how-to-convert-for-tax-savings/[5]

## Mega Backdoor Roth for Larger Contributions
For high earners maxing 401(k) deferrals (**$24,500** under 50 in example + **$15,000** employer match), add after-tax 401(k) contributions (up to **$32,500** room) and convert in-plan to Roth if plan allows; requires plan support for after-tax contributions and in-plan/in-service conversions. Convert promptly to avoid taxable earnings; 2026 Roth catch-up rules add urgency.[2]

**Source**: https://www.westerncpe.com/etax-alerts/the-mega-backdoor-roth-are-your-clients-leaving-tax-free-money-on-the-table/[2]

- Time conversions in low-income years (e.g., sabbatical) for savings; use closing market price on conversion day.[5]
